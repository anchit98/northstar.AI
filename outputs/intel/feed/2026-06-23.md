#### Up there a vehicle earns its keep as a heated shelter that happens to have wheels. You keep its heart beating for the same reason you keep your own. Anyone who has watched their car refuse to start on a freezing morning already understands half of why. Now drop it to -30, and three things hit a shut-off engine at once. The battery loses around 60% of its cranking power at 0F, and an Arctic winter sits well below that. At that same moment a diesel needs 2 to 3 times more power to turn over, because cold metal bleeds away the compression heat it relies on to fire. You are asking for triple the output from a battery delivering a third of it. Then the fuel quits. Standard diesel carries paraffin wax that stays dissolved when warm and crystallizes into gel around 0 to 10F. That wax plugs the filter and the lines, so even a cranking engine gets nothing to burn. Newer trucks add a second trap: the exhaust fluid freezes near 12F, and the engine is built to refuse to start without it. So shutting off at -30 is not a pause. It can be a one-way decision. The block cools, the oil thickens to syrup, the fuel waxes, and the machine stops being a vehicle until something thaws it. Out there, that something might be spring. The unlocked door is for the bear: somewhere to dive into when a 1,500 lb predator that genuinely stalks people for food rounds a building. The running engine is for the cold. The bear is the reason for the headline. The cold is the reason for the rule.

#### A company just announced it's sunsetting jpeg. Relax, your photos are safe. Different jpeg. The one that matters shipped in 1992 and quietly runs the heaviest load on the internet. Humanity takes about 2.1 trillion photos this year, 5.3 billion a day, 61,400 every second, and the overwhelming majority become JPEGs. An estimated 14 trillion of them sit on phones, servers, and drives right now. The trick that won is almost rude. JPEG slices your image into 8x8 pixel blocks and rebuilds each block as a sum of 64 cosine waves. Then it deletes the high-frequency waves your eye can barely register and keeps the low-frequency ones you actually see. Your kid's face becomes math, the math gets trimmed exactly where you won't notice, and the file drops to a tenth of its size with nothing visibly lost. The engine underneath is the discrete cosine transform, built in a UT Arlington lab in 1974. Nasir Ahmed proposed it, had the funding turned down, and developed it anyway. That one transform now powers MP3, MPEG video, the H.26x codecs behind every stream you watch, and AAC. A single insight about which details a human can afford to lose, compressing most of the media you touch. It never had a logo or a founder writing a teary thread. It just sits under nearly every image you've ever seen, and it's going absolutely nowhere.

#### OpenAI's hottest new product is Codex. The whole thing runs on 43 people across engineering, product, and design, covering 10 to 12 separate product surfaces. At a normal company each of those surfaces gets its own squad of 15 to 20. Same scope, 150 to 240 people. People assume the gap is speed: AI makes each engineer 5x faster, so you need fewer of them. That's the small part. The bigger part is what most of those 150 people were actually doing. In a 20-person squad there are 190 possible communication links between people. In a 3-person team there are 3. Every person you add multiplies coordination, and it compounds until 30 to 50% of a traditional team's total effort goes to keeping everyone in sync. So you hire to manage the coordination you created by hiring. EMs and TPMs and scrum masters to run the engineers. Specialists once the work gets complex. Managers for the specialists. Each layer solves a problem the previous layer caused. AI did more than speed up the building. It removed the reason the pyramid existed. When an agent absorbs the mechanical work, the 17 roles that coordinated the 3 builders have nothing left to coordinate. Cursor ran this to the end: 40 engineers, one PM, past $2 billion in ARR faster than almost any company in history. The left side of this chart was never the product team. It was the overhead you paid to move one product team slowly.

#### What decides whether you're still independent at 80 is one capacity almost nobody trains: how fast you can produce force. Strength is force. Power is force times speed. They feel identical until you trip. When you stumble, you have under half a second to fire a correcting step before you go down. That save is a power movement. You can leg-press 200 pounds and still hit the floor, because the press is slow and the recovery is not. The mechanism sits in your nervous system. Power lives in type II fibers, the fast-twitch ones, and they're wired to large, fast motor neurons. Those neurons die off first with age and disuse. As they go, the fibers they controlled lose their signal and shrink. The muscle gets smaller, and the wiring that made it fast goes with it. That's why power drops about twice as fast as strength after fifty. Strength fades slowly. Speed falls off a cliff. And when researchers test which one predicts who falls, who keeps living alone, and who dies sooner, power beats strength every time. The trap is that the obvious fix makes the gap worse. Slow grinding reps build the slow fibers and barely wake the fast ones. You can train hard for a year, feel stronger, and still have a body that can't react in time. The fibers that catch you when you fall are the first ones you stop using. So use them on purpose. Move something light as fast as you can. Jump down off a low step and stick the landing. The body keeps what you ask it to keep.

#### My biggest takeaways from Claude Code/Cowork lead @Nerdi_Yogi: 1. When your engineers ship 8x more code than a year ago (like they do at Anthropic), the biggest problem becomes verification. How do you know that the experience you shipped is what you intended? One tactic Fiona’s team uses is a “bad vs. sad” tracking framework: bad is unrecoverable errors (like a crash), sad is a recoverable pain point (like flickering or drop in conversation). They give each team agency to build and ship quickly, but they track bad and sad events for their surface areas to quickly identify issues. 2. As engineers increasingly work independently with their own teams of agents, loneliness is emerging as a challenge for engineers. To help, Fiona’s team started “pairwise programming lunches,” where engineers work side by side, not necessarily on the same thing, and pick up new patterns by watching how others use Claude Code and Cowork. 3. Anthropic built a dashboard that counts how often users swear at Claude Code. Back in September, amid visible user frustration, an engineer suggested tracking swear words, and Fiona loved it. It’s become a proxy for things evals struggle to capture: whether the experience is actually delightful, not just technically accurate. 4. Look for latent demand to discover new business opportunities. Cowork emerged when the team noticed that non-coders were using Claude Code for tasks like analyzing MRIs or recovering wedding photos. That signal—people jumping through hoops to make something work with your product—tells you there’s something there. 5. Fiona’s team has shifted from six-month roadmaps to just-in-time monthly planning. She tried doing lightweight six-month roadmaps when she joined Claude Code, but a few months in, she realized her team had barely referenced them. Now they do monthly planning on a simple spreadsheet with a simple list of this month’s priorities. 6. One of Claude Code’s cultural values is: you have permission to kill any process that isn’t working. Fiona brought in six-month planning from her previous experience, then killed it herself when it wasn’t serving the team. Always ask: is this process still serving its purpose? 7. Another core principle: “What’s better than me doing it? Having Claude do it.” This pushes everyone to keep checking whether a task can be automated—even writing the post that announces a model launch. Fiona admits that after decades of shipping software by hand, she still has to remind herself to ask it. 8. When Fiona hires managers, they have to start as individual contributors. This gives them time to learn the codebase, build rapport with the team, and understand what it’s like to be an engineer on the team before taking on management responsibilities. It prevents the trap of immediately reaching for your “manager toolbox” instead of learning the specific context. 9. Fiona uses “routines” to automate her daily rituals as a manager. She used to read user feedback channels over coffee and hand-pick fixes to assign teammates, Now a Claude “routine” runs every morning, kicking off agents that analyze feedback across multiple channels, identify themes, and generate PRs to address issues. Her prediction is that work is shifting from manual synchronous prompting to asynchronous agent management (i.e. loops). 10. Culture, not code, is what keeps her up at night. To Fiona, culture is a living thing, not a poster on a wall. Her nightmare is the manager who says, “everything’s fine” while the room is on fire. She pushes hard for open talk about what’s not going well, because that’s the only way the team can fix it together.
