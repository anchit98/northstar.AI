#### Second place on a hard benchmark can mean two very different things. Either you built the capability yourself, or you trained on the model that did. Which problems Kimi 2.7 got marked correct points hard at the second one.

Stop reading the ranks and read which problems each model actually solved. Fable 5 and Kimi 2.7 both get problems 1, 3, 4, 5, and 7 accepted. Every other model on the board, GPT-5.5, Qwen 3.7, the older Kimi 2.6, stalls at 1, 4, 5, and 7. Problem 3 is the discriminator. Only the two top models cracked it, and one of them shipped months after the other.

Two strong models converging on the same easy problems proves nothing. Two models converging on the same hard problem that stumped everyone else is a fingerprint. That is what distillation leaves in the data. Independent reasoning does not line up this cleanly on the exact problem nobody else could touch.

The benchmark author's own note says the quiet part. Kimi 2.7 "loses to Fable mainly on coverage." A model that mirrors the leader's strengths one notch lower across every column is what you get when you train on the leader's outputs. You inherit the ceiling and a little of the noise underneath it.

Honest caveat: a smoke test logs accept or reject, not the proof path. If Kimi 2.7 reached Problem 3 through different reasoning than Fable, the read weakens. The pattern raises the question. It does not settle it.

Here is what the "amazingly good" framing buries. If the number two model is a distilled copy of number one, the leaderboard is compressing the real gap. The distance between the lab that built the capability and the labs racing to mirror it looks like a single rank. It is actually the difference between writing the proof and copying it.

#### An 83-year-old retired art teacher is filling sketchbooks at an Indiana lodge, and the reason his hand moves this fast is a career spent drawing the one room in America where cameras are still banned.

His name is Steve. He taught art in Terre Haute his whole life and for years he worked as a courtroom sketch artist.

That job exists because of one rule. In 1944, Rule 53 banned photography in federal courtrooms, and it still holds today, including the Supreme Court. When a federal trial reaches the news as a drawing, that drawing is the only image the public will ever get. One pass. No photo to work from later.

So the skill a courtroom artist builds is unforgiving. You get a single look at a live scene that will not hold still. You catch the geometry of the room, the posture, the thing on a face a lens would flatten, and you finish before the moment is gone. Hours of a moving room compressed into one frame.

That eye does not retire. It is the same eye now landing on a creek, a tent, a campsite, laundry strung between two trees. Every page drawn from life, in one pass, the way he was trained.

His wife died a few months ago. So he drives to a lodge in Spring Mill and draws.

Drawing from life is the one task that will not let your mind wander off. The line goes where your eye actually points or it goes wrong. You cannot render a tree while you are somewhere else. For a man carrying that much, the most useful thing he ever learned might be the discipline that holds him exactly where he's sitting.

#### Every snowflake landing on that dish carries more energy than radio astronomy has pulled out of deep space in its entire history.

That isn't a figure of speech. Carl Sagan ran the number on Cosmos in 1980, after Frank Drake handed it to him: add up every radio photon ever caught by every radio telescope on Earth since 1933, and the total comes out below the kinetic energy of one snowflake hitting the ground. About a millionth of a joule. Drake later joked we might be up to two snowflakes by now.

A radio photon carries roughly a millionth of the punch of a photon of visible light. The signal from a distant quasar lands at about a quadrillionth of a watt. You cannot point a small antenna at that and hope. You have to build a bucket.

This is the bucket. 64 meters of parabolic steel, 3,800 tons, sitting near Kalyazin north of Moscow. It swings a full 360 degrees and tips past vertical, and the reflecting surface has to hold its shape to within a fraction of the wavelength it's listening for the entire time it moves. Through wind. Through summer thermal expansion. Under the snow you're looking at.

The reason it has to be this enormous is the same reason it catches so little. Radio wavelengths run from centimeters to tens of meters, thousands of times longer than light, and at those wavelengths aperture is the only lever that exists. Bigger dish, sharper image, more photons in the bucket. No optical trick substitutes for raw collecting area.

What this specific dish reached for is the part that gets skipped. In 1983 it pulled radar back from Venus and helped build a thermal map of a surface no camera can see. In 1986 it tracked the Vega probes through the tail of Halley's Comet. It listened for Phobos out at Mars. Decades of the faintest signals humans have ever deliberately chased, gathered by a machine that across all of it collected less energy than the snow now piled on its rim.

The most sensitive instruments we know how to build are the ones that catch almost nothing. That is the entire job.

#### http://Customer.io's CEO built a Slack bot that audits every new strategy doc against the company's 20 to 50 ratified documents.

They call it Chiefy.

Here's the problem it solves. Every company has a corpus of "official" docs. The operating model, the strategy, the pricing philosophy. Then someone publishes something new, gets alignment, and three months later half of those docs quietly disagree with each other.

Nobody catches it because nobody has time to re-read two dozen documents every time one changes. That's why Notion goes stale.

Chiefy runs the audit automatically. Two use cases:

1. You create something new, like an analysis or a pricing doc. Run it through Chiefy and it flags discrepancies against the ratified corpus before it goes to other execs.

#### Boarding back to front feels efficient and it's the reason you're still standing on the jet bridge. It loads an A320 in 31 minutes. The fastest known method does it in 11.

Boarding speed is a luggage problem. A plane fills only as fast as people can lift bags into bins, and just a few can do that at once without standing in each other's swing space. Walking to your seat is a rounding error.

Load the last rows first and twenty people pack into the back of the cabin reaching for the same bins, each one blocking the person behind, while the front half sits empty. Back to front nails the variable that barely matters and wrecks the one that decides everything. That's how the tidiest looking method becomes the slowest.

The fix came from Jason Steffen, an astrophysicist who measures planets orbiting other stars. He got stuck in a jet bridge in Seattle in 2005, went home annoyed, and pointed his exoplanet optimizer at the problem. The answer: seat everyone in line exactly two rows apart. 12A, 10A, 8A, 6A. Now nobody is ever stowing a bag in front of the next person, so the maximum number of bags go up at once and the aisle never clogs. A 2011 test with real volunteers and real carry-ons confirmed it.

Even random boarding (17:59) beats back to front, by pure accident. Scatter people and they spread out, so more bags go up at once.

No airline runs Steffen for the same reason it works. It means splitting up families and boarding strangers one at a time in a fixed order, and a gate full of people won't comply. So the math sits on a shelf and we keep boarding the slowest way because it looks the most organized.
