#### The PM role is splitting into two jobs right now. One group spends their day responding to Slack messages: "Can you send the Gong recording?" "How many customers asked for this?" "What's the business impact?" Back and forth. Four messages to get one feature request properly documented. The other group automated that entire loop. Every incoming feature request hits a structured intake. Customer impact, account context, request history, already filled in. The PM sees a triaged queue, not a thread. The split is already happening at $100M AI startups running their entire company out of Claude Code. The 3-step path to get there is simpler than most PMs think. Start with the one workflow your team repeats that nobody should be doing manually. For most PM teams, that's the feature request triage. Every sales rep, every support agent, every CSM pings the same channel and asks the same four questions. You answer them, route the request, repeat. Step 1: automate what you ask. Build the intake form. Force structured input before the request ever reaches you. Step 2: automate the routing. Once the intake is structured, the triage logic can be codified. Which PM owns this? Which team? What priority level? The judgment you've been applying manually becomes a rule. Step 3: turn the rules into an agent pipeline. The playbook you wrote for the Slack bot becomes the foundation of a system that handles the entire first pass without you. What this tells you about the Company OS framework: the AI isn't replacing PM judgment. It's capturing it. When you write the rule that says "if account revenue is over $500K and this feature was requested 3+ times, route to me directly," you've just documented a decision you were making in your head anyway. The OS runs on your judgment, codified once and executed at scale. The PMs building these systems now aren't working harder. They're deciding once instead of deciding repeatedly. Build the OS.

#### Same $1M. One column buys 10 full-time employees. The other buys 2 employees and 10 agents. This is the chart most people read wrong. The instinct is to look at the right side and think "cut 8 people, pocket the savings." That misses the actual move. The budget never shrank. It stayed at $1M. The composition flipped. Salaries dropped from 100% of the cost to 20%. Tokens climbed from $0 to $800K. For the first time in roughly 30 years of software, payroll has stopped being the dominant line item in a tech business. Inference took the top spot. Treat that as a cost-cut and you fumble the whole opportunity. The 2-person team produces several times more output on the same $1M. The agents supply the leverage. Same spend, multiplied throughput. The proof is already public. Cursor runs about 40 engineers and one PM and crossed $2B in ARR faster than almost any company in history. Codex scoped a modernization project a large enterprise had budgeted at 10 engineers for 12 months. Two engineers with agents shipped it in three days. Jensen put a number on the new math. Pay an engineer $500K, and if they haven't burned at least $250K in tokens by year end, something is wrong. Token spend became the proxy for how much you actually delegated. A low number now means an expensive human is doing work an agent should own. The companies that win the next decade keep the $1M and ask a different question: what can 2 people and a fleet of agents build with it? The budget stayed flat. The ceiling on output didn't. #### I keep telling PMs the new interview is a screen share. A CPO just confirmed it on the record. JZ runs product at Laurel with 5 PMs and 4 designers. She used to manage hundreds of people. The team didn't shrink because times got hard. It shrank because one PM can now ship a full front-end and back-end feature to production with an agent, and adding bodies just adds coordination cost. So when she hires, she doesn't ask if you're "AI-native." She asks you to share your screen and show her. Then she grades you on 4 levels: Level 1: You talk to ChatGPT or Claude in a chat window. Ask a question, get an answer. Basically search. Level 2: You've automated a workflow. The same email, the same triage, the same call prep now runs without you. Level 3: You build apps. Something is tedious, so you build the tool that kills it. Level 4: You ship shared apps other people use, or you ship straight to customers. Her finding after running this for every role: most people who call themselves AI-native are level 1. They've read the threads. They've never built the workflow. That gap is the whole interview now. You can write "AI-native" on a resume. You can quote the last post you saw. But the second your screen is up, there's nowhere to hide. Either there's an automation, an agent, an app you actually made, or there isn't. The PMs who clear levels 2 and 3 are doing the work of a team of ten. That's why her org is nine people and still shipping. JZ walked through the whole system on the podcast, including the company OS her team runs out of Claude Code. The screen-share interview is the part that's going to age the best.

#### The 1% vs 99% problem in AI adoption comes down to knowledge trapped in the wrong place. Right now, at every company, 1-2 people in each function have figured out how to use AI well. They built their own workflows. They're saving hours a day. The other 98% don't know what to use or when, so they default to prompting from scratch and getting mediocre output. The gap compounds every week. The fix is architecture, not training. Laurel, a $100M startup, built a GitHub repo where every business function has its own folder: customer success, design, engineering, finance, legal, marketing, product, sales. Inside each folder are subfolders for every activity that function does. Inside each subfolder is a skill file. How to run a renewal. How to prep a handoff. How to do a session brief. Exactly as the best person on the team would do it. Those skill files get uploaded to Claude at the company level, in org settings, under Skills. Every employee sees the same skills. Every employee gets a Slack briefing each morning that routes them to the right skill for their day. JZ, who built this system at Laurel, showed the full structure in this episode. The part that stuck with me: they built an ontology first. Before writing a single skill, they mapped every function's work to categories, then tasks. Marketing maps to X, sales maps to Y, product maps to Z. That sequence matters. You cannot build skills for work you haven't mapped. Most companies try to scale AI adoption bottom-up. Encourage experimentation. Hope the good workflows spread. They don't. The 1% builds in isolation and the gap widens. Top-down works. Map the work, then build the skills, then deploy at the org level. The 1%'s knowledge stops being tacit and becomes infrastructure. The PM ontology is shifting fastest. The guest said it directly: PMs should be thinking like engineers now. The repeatable work, stakeholder writing, competitive analysis, feedback synthesis, is being automated. The PMs who are safe are at companies that mapped this work before it disappeared. Map the work first.

#### Finance spent 30 years building systems of record. The paradox is that the best signal was never in the record. It was in the edits. This is Sarah Guo's private correctness problem. The clean final memo rarely shows how a senior person thinks. The redlines do. "A document is the output of judgment with the judgment removed." That line is the whole problem. The markup from the MD at 11pm is worth more than the polished page it produced. That is why "interviews lie, edits don't" lands. People explain their standards poorly. They reveal them when they change real work. A system of record tells you what the firm knows. A system of judgment tells you what the firm decides is good. For AI in law and finance, the judgment is in the diff. Call it revealed judgment: decision-grade signal you can reuse. Software kept its diffs. Law and finance never built their git. Everyone spent 30 years building the first one. My bet: the second one decides the next decade.

#### A 10-episode anime about a street kid dying in Night City saved one of the most disgraced game launches in history. Now they're doing it again. Cyberpunk 2077 launched in December 2020 as the most anticipated game in years. Within weeks it was pulled from the PlayStation Store, Sony and Microsoft were offering refunds, and class action lawsuits accused CD Projekt of deceiving investors. The game sold 13.7 million copies on momentum alone, then went quiet. September 2022. Studio Trigger released Edgerunners on Netflix. 10 episodes, standalone story, no prior game knowledge required. Steam concurrent players went from 10,000-15,000 to a peak of 136,724 in a single week. Cyberpunk 2077 crossed 20 million copies sold days after the premiere. CD Projekt had its best third quarter in company history. The CFO said so directly: the anime "visibly affected unit sales." That's Polish corporate understatement for "a 10-episode cartoon rebuilt our stock price." What Trigger actually did was harder than it sounds. They took IP from a studio that had just committed the cardinal sin of shipping an unfinished game, made it accessible to people who'd never touched the game, and made existing players fall back in love with Night City. The anime's ending hit hard enough that a generation of players went back just to find the Easter eggs CDPR buried in the game as references. When the sequel was announced at Anime Expo last year, Cyberpunk 2077 jumped from 45,000 concurrent Steam players to 64,000 in a day. In a game that came out in 2020. The sequel has a new director, new cast, same studio. The question now is whether Trigger can pull off the same trick twice: use 10 episodes of animation to make a five-year-old game feel new again. Based on what happened last time, the smart bet is yes.

#### Venezuela just experienced something that statistically should not happen: two of the four largest earthquakes in its recorded history, 39 seconds apart. In over a century of records, the biggest quake the country ever logged was a 7.7 in 1900. Wednesday's 7.5 now ranks second all-time. The 7.2 that struck 39 seconds before it ranks around fourth. Same fault, same minute. That gap is what turned it catastrophic. A building doesn't reset between shocks. The first cracks the structure, shifts load paths, loosens connections. The second lands on a frame that's already compromised. A structure that rides out a single 7.5 can come down under a 7.2 followed by a 7.5. The damage compounds in a way neither quake does alone. The stacked energy is also why it reached so far. Shaking was felt into Colombia and reportedly as far as Brazil's Amazon, over 1,000 miles out. A country's second- and fourth-biggest earthquakes ever, 39 seconds apart. Most fault systems don't do that in 10,000 years.

#### 📈New milestone: crossed 5000+ students in the past 3.5 years over 3 different live courses I’ve taught. I absolutely love teaching and I often get the question “but Shreyas, don’t you feel the itch to do something that has greater impact than teaching?” It is a valid question (as in, I don’t blame whoever might be asking or wondering), but it comes from a place of not seeing what I see: thousands of highly talented leaders who, every day, are applying what they’ve learned to their products, their strategies, their proposals, their leadership approach, and their careers. I don’t do this work primarily for the impact (something I learned only in my 40s: when I do it for love and from a place of mastery in what I do, the impact takes care of itself). But every day, I get notes from students about the staggeringly compounding impact on their companies, teams, and careers. Which means I get to smile every day 🙌 To every student who’s been part of this journey thus far: thank you for your trust, affection, and for sharing your experience 🙏
