### Aakash Gupta

#### On this week's episode, my guest built a working PM agent live, on camera.

Here's what it did.

It connected to a GitHub repo. It pulled 40 discussions, 60 issues, and 8 releases. It scored every single item for importance. Then it handed back a report: the top pain points, the feature requests that matter, the themes worth shipping, and a game plan to start the day with.

That's a PM's entire Monday morning. Done before the PM sits down.

Now look at what it took to set up.

Create a GitHub repo. Add your Anthropic API key. Log into GitHub. Three steps. No engineering background required.

That's the part worth sitting with.

The most tedious part of product management has always been triage. Reading through scattered issues, discussions, and requests. Holding all of it in your head. Figuring out what actually matters this week versus what is just loud.

An agent can now do the collection and the first-draft scoring for you. The judgment stays yours.

But here's the detail that separates a real PM agent from a toy.

My guest didn't just trust the scores. They wanted an eval that checks the agent's scoring against how they have historically prioritized bugs and feature requests. The agent learns your taste. Then you verify it actually captured your taste.

That's the full loop. The agent triages. The eval keeps it aligned to how you actually think.

This is the "PMs becoming builders" shift, made concrete. A PM spins up an agent that absorbs the grunt work. The human keeps the hours that matter, the decisions only a human should make.

The setup took three steps. Two years ago, the same capability would have taken an engineering team a full quarter to build.

If you run a product backlog, this is your weekend project.

#### I asked my guest a question I've been stuck on for months.

When you're evaluating an AI agent, when can you get away with vibe evals, just eyeballing the outputs and trusting your gut? And when do you have to do the painful version: axial coding, where a human annotates every single span and every issue to build a real ground-truth dataset?

Can you start vibey and layer in the rigor later?

The answer reframed how I think about evals entirely.

Vibe evals fall short fast. The problem is that a pure vibe eval has no actual human curating the taste of the agent behind it. The signal-to-noise ratio collapses almost immediately.

But here's the part people get wrong in the other direction.

Even if you do everything right, even if you run full axial coding, hand-annotate every span, and build a beautiful ground-truth dataset, your eval will still drift out of alignment over time.

The data keeps moving. New user behavior. New edge cases. Failure modes you never annotated because they didn't exist yet.

A perfect eval on Monday is a misaligned eval by next month.

So the real workflow looks different from what most people picture:

1. Kick off a simple eval to start
2. Interrogate it. Where do you agree with it? Where do you disagree?
3. Decide whether this is an eval you can actually trust
4. Re-align it against fresh user data, continuously

Most people frame this as a choice between vibe evals and axial coding. The deeper mistake sits upstream of that choice: treating either one as a finish line.

An eval behaves like a living system. It decays the moment you stop maintaining it.

The teams shipping reliable agents all share one habit. They re-align their evals fast, before reality drifts too far ahead of the system they built to measure it.
