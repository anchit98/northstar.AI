#### Lewis just restated two decades of happiness research without naming a single study.

Cornell ran the actual experiment in 2003. Van Boven and Gilovich had people recall a purchase over $100, either an object or an experience, then rate how much it added to their happiness. Experiences won, and the gap held every time once basic needs were covered. The paper ran in the Journal of Personality and Social Psychology and started a whole field.

The reason comes down to three mechanisms.

A new watch resets to baseline within weeks. Researchers call it hedonic adaptation, the treadmill where every upgrade quietly returns your happiness to where it started. The 1978 study on lottery winners found jackpot recipients no happier than a control group a year later. The car, the house, the watch, all of it gets normalized fast.

Possessions also drag you into comparison. Your watch is great until someone wears a better one. An hour sitting past the break in the ocean has nothing to rank against.

Experiences fold into who you are. A follow-up paper carried the title "I am what I do, not what I have." People refuse to trade away experiential memories at far higher rates than physical ones, because selling the memory feels like selling off a piece of the self.

His point about decluttering is the same finding from the other direction. Objects accumulate maintenance, storage, and the low background hum of managing them. Subtract the objects and the managing goes too.

The driver with 105 wins, more than anyone in the history of the sport, landed on the part the labs needed thirty years to confirm. The Rolex depreciates. The morning on the board does not.

#### Mattel took the wrong lesson from Barbie and just spent nearly $200 million proving it.

Barbie did $1.44 billion, so Mattel raided the toy closet and greenlit films for Polly Pocket, Barney, Uno, Hot Wheels, and Rock 'Em Sock 'Em Robots. The thesis was simple: own the IP, print the franchise. Masters of the Universe was the first real swing at it. Nearly $200M to produce, plus a marketing budget that on a film this size usually adds $100M+ on top.

It opened to $29.3M domestic, $54.3M worldwide. A movie at this budget needs around $425M globally just to break even, so He-Man has to nearly 8x his opening weekend across the entire run. And theaters keep about half the gross, so the studio actually pocketed roughly $15M of that debut.

The gross is bad. The audience breakdown is worse. Only 5% of opening weekend was under 12. Only 6% was 13 to 17. The single biggest block, 29%, was aged 45 to 54. The people who bought tickets are the people who played with the toy in 1985.

#### Those drifting dots you can never quite look at directly are shadows. The objects casting them sit inside your own eye.

The vitreous is the gel that fills most of your eyeball, roughly 99% water held together by a fine mesh of collagen fibers. Light passes straight through it on the way to your retina. As you age, the gel liquefies and those fibers lose their even spacing and clump into strands and knots.

When light hits a clump, it drops a tiny shadow on the retina behind it. That shadow is the floater. What you perceive is the dark spot it casts on your own light sensor, one cell layer deep.

This is why you can never catch one. The clump is suspended in liquid a few millimeters in front of the retina, so it tracks your eye instead of holding still. Look up and it drifts up with you, then settles a beat later as the fluid stops moving. Chasing it is chasing your own shadow.

It's also why they only appear against a blank wall or a bright sky. A uniform bright background is the one lighting condition where a faint shadow on your retina has enough contrast to register. Any busy scene drowns it out completely.

The part that gets people: this is chemically guaranteed. Your vitreous contains riboflavin, and decades of light passing through it slowly oxidizes the collagen bonds until the mesh degrades. Given enough years it happens to everyone, no exceptions.

70% see them because the other 30% just haven't noticed the shadows their own eyes have been casting the whole time.

#### A human face on a fish triggers real revulsion, and your brain is doing exactly what it evolved to do.

You have a region called the fusiform gyrus that does nothing but scan for faces and flag anything off about them. It works as a threat detector. A face that is almost right but slightly wrong reads to that system as diseased, dead, or dangerous, so the closer something gets to human without nailing it, the harder the alarm fires. Masahiro Mori named this the uncanny valley in 1970.

Jim Carrey on a fish falls into the valley from both sides. The face is human enough that your brain fully engages and starts auditing every micro-expression. But 1998 motion capture couldn't render eye saccades, the way light scatters under real skin, or the tiny involuntary muscle twitches that tell you a face is alive. You get a moving human face your visual system reads as a corpse, stuck on a body that has no business carrying it.

Warner Bros paid around $10 million to learn this. The lesson was already 28 years old.

Then the industry kept repaying the tuition. Polar Express sank $165 million into dead-eyed mocap humans in 2004. Cats spent roughly $95 million on human-feline hybrids in 2019. Same wall, every time.

The AI video labs are standing at the edge of that exact valley right now. Generated clips look flawless until you watch the eyes for three seconds. Limpet was the first big check written against a problem still unsolved nearly 30 years later.

#### The whole pitch on these is the "0g added sugar" badge. The sweetener used to hit that zero is the part worth understanding.

Look at the Core Power labels in this grid: sucralose and acesulfame-potassium. In 2022 the Weizmann Institute ran a randomized trial, 120 healthy adults, doses below the accepted daily intake, two weeks. The sucralose group's glucose tolerance measurably worsened. So did the saccharin group. Glucose handling, degraded by the additive that replaced the sugar.

The mechanism runs through your gut bacteria, not your pancreas. Sucralose has almost no calories because your small intestine barely absorbs it, so most of it reaches your colon intact. There it shifts which microbes dominate and changes what they secrete into your bloodstream. The researchers proved cause by transplanting the humans' gut bacteria into germ-free mice. The mice inherited their donors' elevated glucose response.

A separate 2023 NC State study found sucralose-6-acetate, an impurity in the sweetener and also formed in your gut after you drink it, broke DNA in human cells and made the gut wall more permeable in tissue tests. The amount in one daily sucralose drink sits above Europe's safety threshold for DNA-damaging compounds. That work was cells and tissue, not whole people, so weight it accordingly.

One honest correction to the grid: these aren't interchangeable. The Triple Zero uses stevia, which came through that same trial clean. The 25 grams of protein does exactly what the label says.

So the real tell is the chemistry. Sucralose reaching your colon almost fully intact is why it carries zero calories and the same reason your gut bacteria get a vote on your blood sugar. One molecule, both effects.

#### Barcelona. And the seafront you're looking at is one of the most convincing fakes in Europe.

For most of its history this city kept its back to the water. Rail lines, factories, and a shantytown called the Somorrostro sealed off the coast. At its peak 40,000 people lived directly on that sand. The city bulldozed it in 1966.

What sat here was an industrial edge nobody wanted to look at.

Then Barcelona won the 1992 Olympics and used it as cover to gut the entire waterfront. They ripped out the railways, cleared the factories, built the Olympic Village where the towers now stand, and laid down roughly 4 kilometers of beach from scratch. The golden sand was shipped in, some of it from Egypt.

The catch is that the sea keeps trying to take it back. Barcelona loses about 30,000 cubic meters of sand every year to erosion and trucks in more to replace it.

So the most photographed coastline in the Mediterranean is a surface that has to be manually refilled to keep existing. A beach maintained like a swimming pool, sitting on top of a neighborhood that got erased.

The water beyond it is the only part of this picture that was always there.
