#### That scorpion was never real. The entire thing is CGI.

Nvizible, the VFX studio that built it, gave a full breakdown in 2014. Their CG Supervisor Martin Chamney said directly: "A scorpion like this could never be filmed for real." They spent over a year on it. Started with a discreet animation test of a CG scorpion on a human hand, then built the full digital creature from scratch.

What Daniel Craig actually had on his hand was a tiny wire tetrahedron glued together as a tracking prop. Colored plasticine balls on each corner so the software could differentiate front, back, and top when his hand moved. The first version collapsed and all they managed to do was glue their fingers together.

The tracking was the hardest part. Skin stretches when you move your hand, which breaks standard motion tracking. They ran PFtrack for object tracking, then sub-pixel manual tracking in Maya to nail the contact between digital scorpion and real skin.

Skyfall had 1,300+ VFX shots across eight studios. Roger Deakins refused to use green screens because he didn't want green light contaminating his shots. So the VFX teams had to rotoscope practically everything by hand. A $200M production where the cinematographer actively made the digital work harder because he wanted every frame to look real.

The best VFX is the kind where 1.5 million people watch the clip 13 years later and still think it was a real animal behind glass.

#### If you've built AI into your product, check whether it's stuck at the chatbox.

The chatbox was 2023 AI design. Type a question, wait for an answer, go back to what you were doing. Google just published research on replacing it with cursor-based AI. Farza shipped a working version the same week called Clicky. Both are betting on the same thing: AI that has context of your screen, responds where you're working, and never makes you describe what you're looking at.

So how do you actually build this into your product? You score every AI feature against a three-stage rubric.

Stage 1, Destination: user leaves your product to get help. Stage 2, Embedded: AI lives inside the product but the user still manages it. Stage 3, Cursor Layer: AI sees what the user sees and responds where they are.

I ran this audit on a fictional B2B SaaS called TaskFlow. 50K users, AI sidebar that summarizes tasks and answers project questions. Looks like Stage 2. The audit said otherwise.

Their highest-frequency exit: users leaving the Gantt view to describe what they're looking at to the AI sidebar 12 times a week. The AI is 40 pixels away and users still lose context because they have to explain what they see. Stage 1 hiding inside a Stage 2 product.

Their most expensive dead question: "what's blocking this task?" Asked 6 times a day. Each time: click the task, read the thread, click linked tasks, piece it together. 3 minutes. 18 minutes per PM per day on a question the system already has the answer to.

Score each feature. Score = Frequency × Time Saved × Stage Gap ÷ Effort.

Dependency explainer fires 12 times a week. Stage 1 to Stage 3 jump. Every planning meeting picks this first. Score: 5.3. Six quarters to build.

Status update generator fires 3 times a week. Stage 1 to Stage 2. Nobody gets excited about it. Score: 24.0. One sprint.

The 1-sprint fix beats the 6-quarter rewrite.

That's the method for learning from Google and Farza. You don't copy Clicky. You don't rebuild your product around a cursor. You run the audit, find the boring feature that scores highest, and ship it in two weeks. The full audit template and scoring rubric are in the toolkit below.

#### Everyone sells AI agents as a speed boost. My guest's first month building one was 10x slower than just doing the work by hand.

That number is the most useful thing in the episode, once you understand why it happened.

Here is what happened.

My guest wanted an agent to triage on-call outages for them. Outage response is text-heavy, so it seemed like a natural fit for an agent doing knowledge work.

But the first real task exposed the problem. The agent needed to read Slack and figure out which outages were live. The harness simply could not do it.

The shortcut was obvious. Tab over to Slack, copy the messages, paste them into the agent. Thirty seconds of work.

They refused.

Copy-paste finishes the task, but it never teaches the agent to do the full job on its own. So instead of taking the shortcut, they asked a different question. What must I build first so the agent can reach Slack by itself?

This is where it gets interesting.

To give the agent Slack access, they needed credential management built into the harness. To build credential management, they needed keychain access. To handle the keychain safely, they needed secure coding practices to avoid cryptography mistakes with sensitive material.

My guest described recursing six, seven, eight levels deep before hitting the actual missing primitive. You build that primitive, then pop back up the stack one layer at a time, until the original task is finally possible.

That is why month one runs 10x slower.

In that month you are building every foundational capability the feature silently depends on, long before you reach the feature itself.

The teams that quit during this phase conclude that agents do not work. The teams that push through come out the other side with an agent that genuinely does the full job, instead of a human babysitting a copy-paste loop forever.

The slow month is the investment. The shortcut is the trap.

#### The reason AA works when willpower alone fails is one of the most counterintuitive findings in addiction neuroscience.

Shame activates the same neural circuits as physical pain. fMRI studies show the anterior insula and dorsal anterior cingulate light up identically whether you're feeling social rejection or touching a hot stove. Addicts self-medicate to escape that exact pain. Willpower asks them to sit with it longer. AA removes it.

When Brad Pitt walks into a room and says he was on his back, desperate, trying anything, every person in that room experiences a measurable cortisol drop. Researchers at UC Santa Barbara found that hearing high-status individuals disclose vulnerability reduces cortisol in listeners by up to 18%. Your brain literally recalculates "how dangerous is it to be honest here" in real time.

That recalculation is the entire mechanism. Addiction researchers have known since the late 1990s that shame is the single strongest predictor of relapse. Stronger than craving. Stronger than environmental triggers. The addict who believes they're uniquely broken drinks to escape that belief. AA doesn't fight the craving. It dissolves the belief underneath it.

Dax nailed the framing. You don't walk into that room because things are working. You walk in because the pain of hiding finally exceeds the pain of being seen. And then you discover the room already knew.
