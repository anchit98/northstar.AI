#### Argentina deleted 13 zeros from its currency between 1970 and 1992. One modern peso equals 10 trillion of the originals. Which is why the funniest thing about a "peso goes to zero" chart in 2026 is the timing: the peso is currently the most boring it's been in a generation. Every long-term chart of a high-inflation currency looks exactly like this. A cliff, then a flatline. The flatline isn't stability, it's the resolution of a linear axis. Once a currency loses 90%, every subsequent move gets crushed into pixels. Put the same data on a log scale and the actual story appears: the steep part ended two years ago. Here's what the flatline is hiding. Annual inflation peaked near 300% in early 2024. It's 33.5% now. The peso trades around 1,487 to the dollar with weekly volatility of about 0.4%, tighter than some G20 currencies. Argentina scrapped its currency bands in April 2025, let the peso float against a $20B IMF program, and the float held. None of this means the peso is a good savings vehicle. Argentines still price apartments in dollars, and 33% inflation would be a five-alarm crisis anywhere else. But a MAX chart on a linear axis is a machine for telling you about 2002 and 2018 while looking like it's telling you about today. The crash on that chart is real. It's also already over. Reading it as breaking news is like pulling up a 20-year chart of Blockbuster in 2026 and announcing the DVD market is collapsing.

#### The biggest eye any animal has ever grown belongs to the blue whale. It's the size of a grapefruit on a body 100 feet long, and it's colorblind. Underwater, a cornea is nearly useless. Water and the fluid inside your eye bend light by almost the same amount, so the front of the eye, which does about two thirds of the focusing on land, stops working as a lens the moment you submerge. Fish solved this with a hard, round lens that does all the focusing itself. Whales, whose ancestors walked on land carrying the flat lens of a mammal, re-grew that spherical fish lens after they went back to the sea. The eye in a photo like this is a land mammal's eye rebuilt on a fish's blueprint. Then they turned it into a light bucket. A blue whale dives past 1,000 feet, into water where sunlight is nearly gone, so the eye lines its entire back wall with a mirror. 50 to 70 stacked layers of collagen bounce every stray photon back through the retina for a second pass. Land mammals with night vision carry this mirror across the top half of the eye. Whales coat the whole sphere. That's what flashes when a dive light catches a whale in the dark. Color was the price. Down where the last light is dim and blue, cone cells earn nothing, so whales deleted them. The gene for blue cones is broken in every living cetacean. In baleen whales the red-green gene is broken too. Whatever animal this is, it sees the ocean in a single channel of grey. And it can barely see you. The newest modeling of humpback vision puts fine detail only within about 45 to 60 meters. Past that, the largest animals that have ever existed move through a blur and run on sound instead of sight. So the eye that looks like it's reading your soul is a colorblind, nearsighted fish lens on a 150-ton mammal, tuned to catch the last blue photons a thousand feet down. To the whale, you're a warm grey smudge at the far edge of what it can resolve. Every part of it traces back to one move 50 million years ago, when its ancestors left the land. The eye is the invoice.

#### The Army already ran this experiment. On real soldiers. Sustained combat operations, sleep deprivation, and calorie restriction can crash a soldier's testosterone by up to 65%. Healthy men in their 20s, hormonally, temporarily resembling a hypogonadal senior citizen. This isn't a training effect. It's an endocrine one: the body deprioritizes reproduction and muscle synthesis to keep the brain and heart running on limited fuel. So Army researchers tested the obvious fix. In the Optimizing Performance for Soldiers study, healthy men got 200mg of testosterone enanthate weekly during a simulated 28-day deployment deficit. The hormone worked exactly as advertised: the treated group preserved significantly more lean muscle mass than the group left to crash naturally. Physical performance declined at nearly the same rate in both groups anyway. Muscle mass and physical output turned out to be two different systems. Testosterone protected one. The stress of sleep loss and caloric deficit degraded the other regardless of hormone levels. The follow-up study, now underway, swapped the weekly injections for a single long-acting shot and added cognitive testing. The open question the Pentagon is funding right now: whether restoring a hormone can restore combat capability, or whether it just restores the physique that combat capability is supposed to come from.
