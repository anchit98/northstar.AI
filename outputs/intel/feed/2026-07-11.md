#### A Barcelona airport McDonald's is charging €1.70 for a cheeseburger and €5.20 for the double. The second patty costs twice as much as the entire first burger, bun and cheese included. That gap is one of the most studied tricks in retail pricing. Supermarkets built the playbook decades ago. Shoppers can't track 30,000 prices, so they judge whether a store is expensive from a handful of reference items they buy constantly: milk, eggs, bananas. Retailers pin those at or below cost and load the margin onto everything nobody price-checks. McDonald's version of milk is the plain cheeseburger. Every customer carries a price memory for it, so it stays near street price even inside an airport. The double has no reference price in anyone's head. It absorbs the full airport markup. And the company knows its component costs to the cent. In 2008, when it created the McDouble by dropping one slice of cheese from the double cheeseburger, a Wall Street analyst calculated the change saved six cents per burger. The extra patty and cheese on this Barcelona double cost McDonald's maybe 40 cents. The kiosk charges €3.50 for them. Run the arbitrage: three cheeseburgers cost €5.10 and come with three patties, three slices of cheese, three buns. The double costs €5.20 and comes with two of each. Ordering the cheaper option gets you more food. The trick survives because almost nobody comparison-shops within a single menu. You see the €1.70 anchor, conclude the whole board is fair, and pay €5.20 for €3.40 worth of burgers.

#### Most AI agents do not crash when they fail. They quietly hallucinate a fact or grab the wrong tool, and you only find out when a user tells you. Meng To ran into this while building his own AI products, including a job search tool with 16 different agents running inside it. Everything passed in testing. Then real users started hitting problems the tests never caught. The fix came down to visibility. Trace every tool call and every decision the agent makes, the same way engineers trace a request through a backend system, then evaluate where it actually breaks. One trace caught an agent that was supposed to pull a company's tech stack straight from the job posting. It said React. The posting said Python. Reading the final output alone would never catch that. Only watching the steps that produced it would. That exact failure was showing up in about 12% of runs. After tracing it, writing evals for it, and shipping a fix, the rate dropped under 2%. The whole loop took about 20 minutes, and now it runs on its own. Software teams stopped shipping code without tests decades ago. Most AI products today are still shipping on faith. Trace everything.

#### That crust coating the base of every spine took 250 million years to show up, because this urchin never evolved a way to stop it. This is a pencil urchin, order Cidaroida. At the end of the Permian, 252 million years ago, an extinction that killed roughly 90 percent of marine species nearly wiped out sea urchins completely. Only two lineages survived into the Triassic: the ancestor of every other urchin alive today, and the ancestor of this one. From there the branches split in how they handled maintenance. Most modern urchins grow living skin over their spines and keep it clean with pedicellariae, tiny pincer organs built for defense and hygiene. Cidaroids kept the older setup. Their spines shed that skin once they finish growing and stay bare for the rest of the animal's life, with nothing left to scrape them. Algae, bryozoans, and sponges take advantage of exactly that gap. They settle directly on the exposed spine surface and stay there, which is the growth visible at the base of every spine in this photo. Every other urchin alive spent 250 million years building a cleaning crew. This one just kept walking around Japanese tide pools wearing the original, unmaintained hardware.

#### The MOAB weighs 21,600 pounds. When it slides off the ramp, the C-130 instantly sheds roughly a sixth of its flying weight, and the wings don't get the memo. Lift depends on airspeed and wing shape. Neither changes in the half second the bomb exits. So for a moment the aircraft generates lift for a plane that no longer exists, and the surplus shoves it upward. That's the jerk you see in the tail camera. The delivery method is the stranger part. MOAB is too big for any bomb bay in the Air Force. So it rides out the back of a cargo plane on a pallet, yanked into the air by a drogue parachute, the same procedure used to airdrop trucks and food. The most powerful non-nuclear weapon in the US arsenal leaves the aircraft exactly like humanitarian aid. Once the chute pulls it clear, the cradle falls away and grid fins steer it by GPS. Inside sits 18,700 pounds of H6 explosive, about 11 tons of TNT yield. The 2003 test at Eglin threw up a mushroom cloud visible 20 miles away. Engineers built it in nine weeks for the Iraq invasion. It has been dropped in combat exactly once, on a tunnel complex in Afghanistan in 2017. Every other flight it has ever taken looked like this one.
