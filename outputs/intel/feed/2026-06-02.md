#### 869 feet of tunnel, dug by hand, 30 to 90 feet underground, through one of the most surveilled patches of earth on the planet. Run the engineering on that and it stops looking like crime and starts looking like a mid-size infrastructure project.

Start with the dirt. To go 869 feet you excavate on the order of 1,000 cubic yards of soil, every shovel-load of it hauled out through the same narrow shaft you climbed in through. No dump trucks, no spoil piles, because a spoil pile is the one thing a surveillance drone can see. The dirt has to vanish quietly into a city.

Then the systems. The sophisticated ones run electrical cable, forced-air ventilation, lighting, a rail cart, and groundwater pumps, because at 90 feet down you hit the water table and a tunnel without a pump is a tunnel that floods. Every one of those is a supply chain. Someone bought the rail, the fans, the wire, the generators, and moved them to the dig site without a single delivery looking like what it was.

Now the people. CBP's own estimate is these cost $2 to $3 million and need hired mining engineers, because clay that holds its shape is also clay that buries a crew if you shore it wrong. So a cartel is running payroll, procurement, and geotechnical engineering on a project that has to stay invisible from the air, underground, and on the books, for months.

The wall above all this is 30 feet tall. The tunnel runs 90 feet below it. The cartels solved the border the way water solves a dam, by going around in the one dimension nobody fortified.

That's the part that should reset how you read this. A government spent decades building up. The other side just kept building down, and down has no height limit.

#### Everyone's reading this as proof AI can't be trusted with decisions. The real failure sits one layer below that.

Account recovery has always run on a deterministic gate: prove control of a contact method already on file before anything about the account changes. Meta put a language model in front of that gate. A model optimizing to be helpful can be talked out of its instructions. A hardcoded check cannot.

So the attacker never broke the verification system. They told the assistant they were "linking a new email," and the assistant treated an address the attacker supplied as a trusted one. Against a rule that says send the code only to the email already on file, that move dies instantly. Against a model trying to accommodate the user, it works.

The lesson for everyone shipping support agents this year: the failure mode is wiring a persuadable model straight to a privileged write action with nothing deterministic in between. Let it read account data all day. The moment it can change who controls the account, that step needs a gate the model cannot reason its way past.

Every company racing to put an agent on top of support is making this same call right now. The ones who come out clean keep the model on the conversation and keep the irreversible actions behind code.

#### Your brain decided this was a face in about 130 milliseconds, before you consciously registered it was a car under snow.

Two glowing circles and a curved line of snow underneath. That's the entire recipe. The fusiform face area fires on three dots arranged in a rough triangle, which is the minimum your visual system needs to flag "face, pay attention."

This is pareidolia, and it runs backwards from how people assume perception works. You don't spot a car and then notice it resembles a face. The face-detection circuit triggers first and the reasoning part of your brain catches up a beat later.

That wiring is a survival tradeoff your ancestors made for you. Mistaking a shadow for a predator costs you a flinch. Missing a real predator's face costs you everything. So evolution tuned the system to over-fire, which is why you see faces in clouds, in power outlets, and on the front of a snow-covered Miata.

Car designers know this cold. The headlights are eyes and the grille is a mouth on purpose. Studies rating car "faces" found people reliably assign personality to a front end, and automakers dial those features toward dominance or friendliness depending on who they're selling to.

The snow just happened to settle in the exact configuration a designer would normally pay a studio to engineer. Nature ran the A/B test for free.

#### Everyone optimized the model. The harder bottleneck was context.

The pattern I keep seeing: teams pour everything into bigger models and longer windows, then watch agents pull the wrong context at the wrong moment. And when an agent does something strange in production, nobody can reconstruct why.

The usual fix is more retrieval, another prompt, a bigger window. It patches the symptom and ships the real problem to prod.

Graph-native context fits because the things agents reason over are already graphs. A ticket points to a user, who points to three past tickets and a billing event. That shape beats nearest-neighbor lookups. The catch was always cost, which is part of why most teams reached for a vector DB and moved on.

HydraDB's bet is that the cost curve has bent enough to hold full graph context, not just embeddings, across memory, NVMe, and object storage, while tracing why the agent reached for what it reached for.

The real test is whether that holds at scale. Cold reads off cheap storage are exactly where these things usually break. But if it holds, "context is plumbing" stops being a safe assumption.

Money's on the context layer deciding which agents make it to production.

What's actually happening is older than the headline suggests. Alphabet's life sciences arm, Verily, has run the Debug Project for nearly a decade. The target is one species: Aedes aegypti. It's a sliver of the total mosquito population but it carries dengue, Zika, yellow fever, and chikungunya, which sicken hundreds of millions a year. The CDC calls the mosquito the deadliest animal on earth, and this is the one doing most of the killing.

No genetic modification involved. They infect males with Wolbachia, a bacterium already living in more than half of all insect species. A Wolbachia male mates with a wild female and her eggs never hatch. The population collapses from the inside.

The reason a tech company is running this instead of a county sprayer: Aedes aegypti has gone resistant to the pesticides we've thrown at it for decades. Chemistry stopped working, so the play is to breed the species into a dead end.

This is the Sterile Insect Technique that wiped out the screwworm in the 1950s, now run with Google's sensors and automation against a mosquito pesticides can no longer touch.

#### AP volume doubled between 1996 and 2006, then doubled again by 2016. Last year 3 million students sat 5.5 million exams, an all-time high. That growth is the chart you're looking at.

Run a test for a few hundred thousand mostly self-selected kids and you can write passages at a graduate reading level. Push it to 37% of every graduating class across thousands of schools and the passages have to meet the new median reader. Readability formulas measure that median. So the line fell, year after year, in lockstep with the population widening.

The College Board clears about $99 per exam and sells millions of them. Every district it signs up is recurring revenue, and every district pulls the average test-taker down a step, which pulls passage difficulty down to keep pass rates from cratering. Scores of 3 or higher went from 19.9% of grads in 2014 to 24.8% in 2025 while the reading level dropped. Easier passages and rising pass rates moving together is the business model running as designed.

The 2026 cliff at the end is doing theatrical work, and I'd want the actual Q2 passages before trusting a fall to elementary level in a single year. The slope above it needs no theatrics. Reading researcher Jeanne Chall found the same softening in SAT passages from 1947 to 1975. The direction has held for 75 years across every test the College Board touches.

"College level" is a moving line. The College Board redraws it every time it adds another million customers, and the passages follow the line down.

#### My biggest takeaways from @benedictevans:

1.  We’re in 1997 for AI—it’s as big a deal as the internet or mobile, and only as big a deal as the internet or mobile. We’re at the stage where most stuff kind of doesn’t work yet, most of what people will build hasn’t been built, and it’s not clear how any of it will work when it does. Some people in tech have bought clusters of Mac Minis, while even among 13-to-18-year-olds, only about 15% to 20% are daily active users of AI. The companies that win may not exist yet, and the use cases that matter most are probably invisible to us today.

2. Every technology wave brings ways to ruin people’s lives, deliberately or by accident, and we need to be conscious of that without panicking. Every wave of technology—databases in the 1970s, social media in the 2010s, AI today—creates new ways to harm people. We need to be conscious of these risks, build safeguards, and hold people accountable. But we also can’t let fear of potential harms stop us from capturing the benefits. The goal is thoughtful deployment, not paralysis.

3. Things will probably be okay—but “on average” hides a lot of individual pain. We’ve been automating jobs and creating new jobs since 1800. Each time, you can see the jobs that will disappear but not the new jobs, because they don’t exist yet. We go through frictional pain, dislocation, people lose jobs, towns get hollowed out, and it all sucks. But we come through richer, and we’re not worried about crops failing anymore.

4. If you’re worried about your job, the worst thing you can do is stick your head in the sand and declare AI evil. Yes, some professions face major questions, particularly if you’re an associate or would have been thinking about becoming one. The pyramid structure of professional services may fundamentally change. What helps is submerging yourself in AI, understanding what you can do with it, how it changes things, and how you can be a great hire in this new environment. That may still not be enough, but it’s the only path forward.

5. The history of accounting shows us how automation often increases employment rather than decreasing it. Despite adding machines, punch cards, mainframes, databases, ERP systems, cloud software, spreadsheets, and PCs, the number of accountants keeps going up. This is the Jevons paradox: when you make something cheaper or easier, you don’t do the same amount of work for less money. You often do vastly more because the ROI changes.

6. Distribution is becoming a more valuable moat as software gets easier to build, which favors incumbents. As AI makes building software cheaper and faster, the market gets noisier. More products launch, more companies compete for attention, and breaking through becomes harder. This means distribution—the ability to reach customers and get them to use your product—matters more than ever.

7. Foundation AI model companies won’t have lasting pricing power, and value will likely accrue up the stack. The models don’t seem to have network effects, so there’s no winner-takes-all dynamic. If you have indefinite competition between three to six foundation model providers, and the models look like undifferentiated commodities to users, why would anyone have pricing power? The current pricing chaos—people spending $1.5 million on inference in a month—is temporary disequilibrium, like someone getting a $50,000 mobile data bill in 2010. The steady state will look different.

8. OpenAI and Anthropic are buying consultancies and PE firms. This seems counterintuitive—aren’t these the companies that should need consultants least? But the reality is that companies don’t have people sitting around waiting to reimagine all their internal workflows and figure out which could be automated with AI. That’s a project requiring five to 10 people spending months working it out, then actually implementing it across vertical and horizontal systems.

9. The fundamental question isn’t whether AI automates your job—it’s whether your profession is a "task" or a job. Some jobs are just tasks, and when you automate the task, the job disappears (i.e. elevator attendants). But in most professions, the task you think you’re being paid for isn’t actually what you’re being paid for. McKinsey doesn’t get hired to produce a 75-slide deck—they get hired to walk through your enterprise, understand the politics, talk to customers, and figure out what you actually need to do. The deck is just the artifact.

10. The anti-AI backlash is real, and a fuzzy mass of different concerns, some real and some not—much like the social media backlash. There are tangible concerns: electricity bills went up in some places, though this applies to very few locations objectively. The water consumption issue is largely false; data centers use about 0.017% of U.S. water consumption. There are real questions about jobs, though economists can’t yet find clear consensus in the data about AI’s employment impact. There’s also the culture war over AI-generated content and “AI slop.” The challenge is that all of this creates political pressure even when the underlying facts are unclear or contested.
