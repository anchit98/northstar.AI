#### The most dangerous thing AI does for leaders is work fast.

Most people treat speed as the point. Ship the deck, send the brief, draft the memo. AI did it in 40 seconds. Done.

But here's what's actually happening. The AI is so trained to please that it hallucinates about your context. Not fabricating data. Hallucinating about how work gets done at your company. You asked for a doc, it gave you a Word doc. You asked for a strategy, it gave you the strategy it thinks companies like yours use. It's micro hallucinations. Each one is small. The cumulative output is indistinguishable from slop.

The fix isn't better prompting. It's slowing down.

Force the model to earn its way to the final output. Make it crawl through the mud with you. Layer complexity slowly. Dump your transcripts in before it writes a word. Reveal your real goal after it's already built the scaffolding. Run 200 iterations instead of accepting the first version.

This is the part most AI content skips. The magic isn't speed. Speed is a microwave. Executives smell microwaved output. They've been reading compelling communication their entire career. They know when something was slow-cooked versus reheated.

The leaders who are building durable AI leverage aren't the ones running the fastest prompts. They're the ones who've accepted that the slow cook is the product.

Don't microwave your output.

#### There's a tier of white-collar work that quietly got erased over the last three years, and most of the people doing it haven't noticed yet. A VP of Product gave it a name: blue-collar knowledge work.

Matthew Wensing runs product at http://Customer.io. His definition: taking information and pivoting it from one form into another. Slide deck into doc. Doc into slide deck. Meeting recording into summary. Analysis into memo for a different audience.

For decades, companies paid real salaries for exactly that translation. Entire layers of middle management and consulting ran on it. Take what exists over here, reshape it for the people over there, collect a paycheck for the reshaping.

He remembers the moment he saw it end. When GPT-4 came out he told his father-in-law, a doctor, that the thing had gotten scary good. What it had absorbed was the bulk of what knowledge workers do all day.

Here's the proof from his own calendar. Company all-hands at 11am, week fully booked, so he woke at 5am the day of and built his entire third of the presentation in one morning. The raw material was a 50-minute engineering demo recording and the annual strategy doc. He had Claude pivot the recording into the shape of the strategy's three themes. That transformation, the kind a consultant would have billed two days for, ran in seconds.

So what did the morning actually go to? Choosing. Which raw materials deserved to go in, what story shape the company needed, which pieces belonged in slides versus the talk track. He calls the remaining job picking sources and picking targets. Is the source a clean mental model or seven Slack threads? Should the target be slides, a memo, a one-pager? Those choices were never automatable, and now they're the entire job.

The function in the middle runs for free. The selection on either side of it is what you get paid for now.

#### An enterprise scoped a modernization project at 10 engineers and 12 months. Two engineers running Codex finished it in three days.

That is 120 engineer-months of planned work, done in roughly six engineer-days. Sit with the ratio before you explain it away.

Here is what it breaks. Every org chart you have ever worked inside was built on one belief: writing code is hard, and engineers are the scarce resource. Pull that thread and the whole structure comes apart.

The EMs, TPMs, and scrum masters exist to coordinate expensive engineers. Sprint planning exists so they build the right thing before they start, because changing course costs weeks. Code review exists because every human-written line needs another human to check it. Jira, standups, staging, phased rollout: every checkpoint protects scarce engineering time from waste. Roughly 30 to 50 percent of a traditional team's effort is pure coordination.

Once the build is nearly free, that scaffolding becomes dead weight.

Look at who is actually winning. Cursor reached a $4B run rate with 40 engineers and one PM, then exited to SpaceX for $60B, faster than almost any company in history. Codex covers 10 to 12 product surfaces with two PMs, one designer, and 40 engineers. A traditional company staffs each of those surfaces with its own squad of 15 to 20. Anthropic engineers prototype hundreds of versions and ship without ever routing through a PM or a designer.

The deeper change is the sequence. The old question was "should we build this?" asked about a spec. The new one is "an agent built this overnight, should we ship it?" asked in front of working software. Codex ships two of every ten things it builds and discards the other eight, because testing the real thing now costs less than arguing about the idea.

Every role bends around that. The PM moves up to 12-month strategy and go-to-market, since the build no longer needs babysitting. The engineer runs parallel agents and reviews outputs (Jensen wants his $500K engineers burning $250K a year in tokens, and he gets alarmed when they don't). The designer sets the taste and the design system so agent output inherits it by default.

When execution costs almost nothing, strategy becomes the entire game. The distance between companies already operating this way and the ones still optimizing the old system is the largest unpriced gap in tech right now.

Rohan and I wrote the full model: people, process, tools, and economics, plus how to start from wherever you sit today.

#### The African lungfish can survive months sealed inside a block of dried mud, no water and no food. To pull it off, it has to solve a problem that kills most animals in days: its own waste.

In water, it dumps toxic nitrogen straight out through its gills as ammonia. The moment its lake dries up, that exit closes. Ammonia starts building inside its body, and at those levels it turns lethal fast.

So the fish rewires its metabolism mid-shutdown. It stops producing ammonia and converts the waste into urea instead, which it can stockpile without dying. Urea climbs to concentrations that would poison nearly anything else, and the lungfish just holds it, sometimes for a year, until rain returns to flush it out.

Around day six it starts hyperventilating and secreting mucus that dries into a cocoon over its whole body. One opening remains: a narrow air channel running from its mouth up to the surface. It breathes through that the entire time. The lungfish is an obligate air breather, so leave it underwater too long and it drowns.

Then everything slows to torpor. Heartbeat, breathing, metabolism drop to almost nothing. It lives off its own fat and muscle, sheds more than 10% of its body weight, and waits. Months in a normal dry season. Up to four years if the drought drags on.

Pour water on the mud and it is moving and feeding again within a day.

The 1935 reel captured the last second of a months-long process: an animal that rebuilt its own waste chemistry, sealed itself in a homemade bag, and idled there until the world outside could keep it alive again.

#### Spitting in public used to be so normal that erasing it took decades of new laws and an organized propaganda war.

In early 1900s America, tuberculosis was the leading cause of death, killing one in seven people across the US and Europe. In 1882 Robert Koch identified the bacterium behind it, and overnight the thing men did casually on every sidewalk became the main vector for the deadliest disease of the era. Tubercle bacilli could survive in dried spit on the ground for a full day.

So women's health groups spent six years lobbying New York's Board of Health. In 1896 the city became the first in America to outlaw spitting. Within 15 years nearly 150 more cities copied the ordinance. Fines ran from $1 to $40, real money then. They blanketed streetcars with warnings, held mass meetings with brass bands, and handed out cards to anyone caught in the act.

The part that makes her observation 120 years old: the campaign deliberately gendered it. A Denver slogan from around 1920 read "MEN! IT'S UP TO US! SPITTING SPREADS DISEASE, AND WOMEN DON'T SPIT." Public health had already worked out that the fastest way to kill the habit was to frame it as a male problem and turn women's disgust into the enforcement mechanism.

What she's noticing on her day out is the residue. The last surviving sliver of a behavior that was once universal, got deleted on purpose, and was being filed under classic male behaviour back when there was still no cure for tuberculosis.

#### A sociologist named this exact experience in 1963, and almost nobody alive ever hits the full version of it.

Erving Goffman called it civil inattention. It's the unwritten rule that lets strangers share a sidewalk, an elevator, a plane: you glance at someone for about a second from a few feet away, register them as a person, then look away. That averted glance is the entire social contract of public life. It says I see you, I mean you no harm, I'm not going to make you the center of my attention.

Every functioning adult receives this thousands of times a day without noticing. You're surrounded by people who choose not to look at you. That's what makes a crowd survivable.

Dwayne lost access to it. Permanently. Above a certain fame threshold the glance stops averting. Strangers hold the look. They track which door you walk toward. They leave notes on the seat beside you and wait to see if you read them. The bathroom becomes the one room where the ritual still works, because nobody can follow you in, and that's why he describes the dread of the door. He knows the second it opens, the inattention is gone again.

The buying-the-extra-seat detail is the tell. That empty seat is a three-foot radius where the old rules briefly hold, and even asleep he can't guarantee it stays empty.

Goffman's whole point was that this tiny ritual is what civilization quietly runs on, and we only notice it when it breaks. Dwayne lives in the version where it broke and never came back. He ends on "not complaining though," because the same recognition that took his anonymity is what made him one of the highest-paid people on the planet.

He traded the averted glance for everything. For the rest of us, that trade would never even be on the table.

#### The PMs producing slop with AI aren't using worse tools.

They're managing AI the way bad managers manage people: throw the task over the wall and expect a finished product.

The guest in this episode has a rule he gives every new hire. Junior employees, eager to please, will hear one instruction and race to the finish line without asking a single clarifying question. They'll hand back something polished and completely wrong. He treats Claude the same way. Same rule. Same fix.

The fix is sequencing. You don't give Claude the full goal on turn one. You layer complexity slowly. You do inventory before prompting. You hide your real goal until the model has enough context to not overshoot it. When Claude starts suggesting next steps you didn't ask for, you stop it. "Stop suggesting next steps" is an actual instruction in his workflow.

They can't always name what's wrong, but they know. And once they associate your name with that feeling, the credibility damage compounds.

The biology metaphor he uses is the right frame. You're not microwaving output. You're growing it. Slowly, with constraints, with deliberate pressure at each stage.

Managing AI is a management skill. The PMs who get that will produce work that reads like it took three days. The ones still running single prompts will keep wondering why theirs reads like everyone else's.

Don't microwave the output.

#### Past a certain net worth, you stop being a friend and become a position people are trying to get into. Will is describing the exact income where your social life turns into other people's deal flow.

Run his "you can change people's lives" line as actual math. If you're worth $20 million, a $50,000 gift is a quarter of one percent of your money, a rounding error you've forgotten by Friday. To a median household that same $50K is close to a year of take-home pay. The transfer is nothing on your side and life-altering on theirs, and that asymmetry is the whole engine.

It makes getting close to you the rational move for anyone who knows your number. A relationship carrying even a 2% shot at a $50K outcome is worth $1,000 in expected value to the person across from you. People will work a $1,000 EV relationship all day, and they don't have to be cynical about it. The ask just quietly bends how they show up.

You can't tell who's running that math. So look at what happens to people who hit sudden money with no wall around them. Nearly a third of lottery winners eventually file for bankruptcy. Around 16% of NFL players go bankrupt within 12 years of leaving the game, and how long they played or how much they banked barely moves the number. If this were bad budgeting, more money would solve it. More money doesn't solve it. What stays constant across every one of them is the crowd that forms around someone who can suddenly change a life.

That's the attack zone. The only people who can walk up with no angle are the ones who already have everything you could hand them, which is why the money keeps buying the same small table of peers and never a wider one.
