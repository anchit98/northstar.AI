#### A single ad agency invented the diamond engagement ring in 1938, and the campaign worked so well we forgot it was a campaign. Diamonds were a collapsing business. The Depression cratered demand, and De Beers sat on enormous stockpiles of a stone that was never rare to begin with. So they hired N.W. Ayer with one brief: tie diamonds to love so tightly that no man could propose without one. In 1939, roughly 10% of American engagement rings had a diamond. By 1990, 80%. A copywriter named Frances Gerety wrote "A Diamond Is Forever" in 1947. The line did double duty. It made the stone a symbol of eternal love, and it made reselling one feel like a betrayal. That second part is the genius. If nobody resells, no secondary market ever exposes the real price. The "two months' salary" rule came from De Beers ads too. And when America was saturated, they ran the identical play in Japan. In 1967, under 5% of Japanese brides received a diamond ring. By 1981, 60%. Demand got manufactured country by country, on a schedule. De Beers controlled up to 90% of global supply for most of the century and released stones slowly to simulate scarcity. Try selling your diamond back today and you'll get 25 to 50% of retail on a good day. The markup is the product. Labs now grow chemically identical stones for a fraction of the price. The industry's answer was another marketing campaign, this time convincing you real love requires the one dug out of the ground. 87 years running. Still the most successful ad campaign in history.

#### Your eye flags the left one as fake before you can explain why, and the reason is one of the most reliable glitches in human perception. Ask a hundred people to scatter dots randomly on a page and almost every one draws the version on the left. Even spacing. Nothing touching. Gaps that all feel about the same size. That's what our brains believe randomness looks like. Actual randomness looks like the right side. It clumps. Some flowers pile on top of each other, some patches sit empty, the spacing is all over the place. This is Poisson clumping, and it's why a genuinely random distribution always produces clusters and voids that feel suspiciously deliberate. The classic case is the London Blitz. Residents were sure the Germans were targeting specific neighborhoods, because the bombs fell in obvious clusters. A statistician mapped every impact and ran the numbers. The pattern came out indistinguishable from random. The clustering was the randomness. Nature runs on the same math. Seeds never land on a grid. They fall where wind and water and root competition drop them, so real grass and flowers grow in clumps and gaps. Your visual system spent a few hundred thousand years learning that evenly spaced anything means something built it. A grid reads as human. A clump reads as wild. Apple learned this the expensive way. The early iPod shuffle used a truly random algorithm, and users flooded support insisting it was broken because the same artist kept repeating. Steve had them rebuild it to be less random so it would feel more random. Spotify made the identical change years later. An elementary art teacher compressed all of that into one rule you never forgot: don't space the flowers evenly. She was training you to draw against your own instinct, because the instinct is the part that reads as fake.
