#### The Odyssey is the strangest possible epic to call masculine. A Victorian novelist wrote an entire book arguing a woman composed it. In 1897, Samuel Butler published his case that the Odyssey was written by a woman. His evidence: the epic's obsession with domestic detail and the fact that women run the entire plot. Athena engineers every step of Odysseus' return home. Penelope holds off 108 suitors for three years with a weaving trick. Nausicaa saves the hero's life on a beach. When Odysseus needs the Phaeacians' help, Athena tells him to skip the king and appeal to Queen Arete instead. Odysseus spends 8 of his 10 lost years detained by two women, Calypso and Circe. The most famous man in Greek literature is a supporting character in his own rescue for most of the poem. Helen's one scene in the Odyssey might be the strangest flex in the ancient canon. She drugs everyone's wine with an Egyptian potion, takes over the storytelling from her husband, and recounts how she alone recognized Odysseus in disguise inside Troy. Menelaus sits there and lets her talk. There's also a problem with scolding Homer at all: he probably wasn't a person. Most classicists treat the epics as centuries of oral tradition, hundreds of anonymous bards refining the same material. Some of them may well have been women. Robert Graves found Butler's theory convincing enough to write an entire novel around it in 1955. Of all the texts in the ancient canon to call masculine, she picked the one where the case for a female author has stayed alive for 130 years.

#### LeBron James turns 42 in December, just committed to a 24th NBA season, and is training with an exercise most players half his age would fall off. Kneeling on an inflated rubber ball, pressing dumbbells overhead. The difficulty is the point: it hammers the one system aging destroys first. Here's the physics. A stability ball gives you two shifting contact points on a surface that rolls in every direction. Adding dumbbells overhead raises your center of mass by more than a foot, turning your body into an inverted pendulum. A 2-degree lean that means nothing on flat ground becomes a torque your muscles have to catch immediately or you're on the floor. Catching it happens below consciousness. Stretch receptors in your muscles detect the drift and fire corrections through spinal reflex loops in under 100 milliseconds. Conscious reaction takes about a quarter second. One set on that ball is thousands of micro-corrections, all running through the reflex circuitry, none through the thinking brain. That circuitry is exactly what age attacks. Fast-twitch muscle fibers atrophy decades before slow-twitch ones. Proprioception, your body's sense of where it is in space, degrades alongside them. It's why elderly people fall, and why aging athletes lose the first step while their bench press barely moves. The strength stays. The correction speed goes. Kareem retired at 42 after 20 seasons. Vince Carter stretched to 43 in a bench role. LeBron just averaged 20.9 points, 7.2 assists and 6.1 rebounds in season 23, then raised it to 23.2 in the playoffs. Aging takes your balance years before it takes your strength. The longest career in NBA history belongs to a guy hammering the thing that quits first.

If pasta made you lean, Italian kids would be the leanest in Europe. They rank near the top instead. Nothing about the diet changed between those two generations. The life around it did. And every piece that vanished was quietly running your blood sugar. Take the walking. Grandma moved all day, to the market, to Mass, to the neighbor's, and the timing did more than the mileage. A contracting muscle drags the GLUT4 transporter to its surface and yanks glucose out of your blood with no insulin involved at all. In a 2025 trial, ten minutes of walking after a sugar load dropped peak blood glucose from 182 to 164 mg/dL against sitting still. Ten minutes. You don't even need to stand up for it. Pumping the soleus in your calf from a chair runs the same machinery. Her grandkids get driven everywhere and feed that muscle nothing, and Italy now tops Europe for children glued to a screen two hours a day or more. The pace of the meal did slower damage. Fullness is a hormonal telegram: GLP-1, CCK, and PYY climbing for roughly twenty minutes before your brain reads the message. A ninety-minute lunch outlasts the delay, so you quit while food is still sitting there. Swallow the same plate alone in ten and you finish before a single one of those hormones fires, then eat past the line on momentum you can't feel. Pool 23 studies and the fast eaters carry 2.15 times the odds of obesity and run nearly two BMI points heavier on identical food. The conversation was doing pharmacological work, and nobody at the table knew it. Order matters as much as speed, which is what kills the "just slow down" advice. Lead with vegetables and the fiber swells into a gel that physically stalls glucose at the intestinal wall, while stimulating GLP-1, the hormone Ozempic borrows, to slow the stomach from emptying. In one crossover study, a 671-calorie meal eaten vegetables-first flattened the 30 and 60-minute glucose spikes even when subjects ate fast. Antipasto, then a little pasta, then the fish is a glucose protocol that beat the glucose monitor to market by three centuries. Two more things caved on top of that. A quarter of Italian children now drink sugar every single day, straight into the blood with nothing to slow it, a source their grandparents didn't have. Meanwhile the home kitchen surrendered to delivery and packaged snacks tuned in a lab to steamroll the same fullness hormones the slow lunch spent ninety minutes protecting. So here is the real trick, and there's nothing secret about it. Move after you eat. Vegetables first. Stretch the meal out. Drink water instead of soda. The grandparents pulled five glucose levers a day by accident. Their grandchildren, raised on the exact same carbs, pull none of them. You can keep the pasta. You cannot skip the life that used to come with it.
