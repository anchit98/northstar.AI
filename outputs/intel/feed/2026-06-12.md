#### The water math on AI is the most misunderstood statistic in tech, so let's actually run it.

A ChatGPT query uses about 0.3 mL of water for cooling, per OpenAI's own disclosure. The harshest academic estimate, which also counts the water consumed by the power plants feeding the servers, lands at 10 to 25 mL. Call it half a tablespoon at the absolute worst case.

Now the comparisons. One hamburger takes roughly 2,500 liters of water to produce. That single burger equals somewhere between 100,000 and 8 million ChatGPT queries depending on whose estimate you trust. A pair of jeans cost 7,500 liters before it reached your closet. One toilet flush is 6 liters, or about 20,000 queries. US golf courses pour out around 1.5 billion gallons every day, dwarfing the direct cooling water of every data center in the country.

So why does the robot demanding a thousand glasses of water feel true? Because the totals sound enormous and nobody does the division. "Data centers consumed billions of gallons" is accurate and terrifying right up until you divide billions of gallons by trillions of queries and get a number smaller than the drop left in your glass when you think it's empty.

The legitimate water story is local. A big facility sited in a drought-stressed basin can strain that specific community, which is why Chile blocked one. That's a siting problem, solvable with policy and closed-loop cooling.

The cartoon will outlive the correction anyway. Per-unit math never beats a thirsty robot at the foot of the bed.

#### The brutal part is what the body does with zero training. It reads inactivity as an instruction and starts disassembling.

The numbers first. 46.5% of US adults meet neither the aerobic nor the strength guideline, and only 26.4% hit both. The bar is 150 minutes of moderate cardio a week plus two strength sessions. Roughly 20 minutes of brisk walking a day and lifting something heavy twice. Most of the country clears neither.

Here's the physiology that runs underneath that. After 30, an untrained body sheds 3-8% of its muscle mass per decade, and the rate accelerates past 60. Muscle is your largest glucose disposal site. Lose it and insulin resistance climbs even at the same body weight. Bone follows the same rule: no mechanical load, no signal to maintain density. The skeleton literally budgets calcium based on how hard you push against gravity.

Then there's the cardio side. A Cleveland Clinic study tracked 122,000 patients and found low cardiorespiratory fitness carried a higher mortality risk than smoking. Smoking. The thing with the warning label on the box.

None of this requires a gym. The dose that captures most of the benefit is a fast walk and two sessions of pushups and squats a week. The body keeps whatever you ask it to keep.

It just never received the request.

#### That can opener in the video will outlive everyone reading this, and the reason it died as a product is pure economics.

It's a single piece of forged steel. One lever, one fulcrum, one blade. No gears, no rivets, no moving parts to wear out. You stab the lid, lever around the rim, done. The only failure mode is losing it.

Modern can openers fail because they replaced that with a gear train. A rotary opener has a cutting wheel, a drive wheel, and a crank, usually cast from zinc pot metal because zinc casts cheap at scale. The teeth on the drive wheel round off after a few hundred cans. The rivet holding the wheels loosens. The cutting wheel dulls and starts crushing the rim instead of slicing it. Three failure modes engineered into a product that used to have zero.

Why did the industry do it? The lever opener takes 30 seconds of technique to learn. The rotary takes none. Easier product wins the shelf, then the shelf demands a lower price every year. Once Walmart sets the category at $3, every manufacturer races to hit it, and the only variable left to cut is metal quality.

So the tool got easier, cheaper, and worse, all at the same time, and each step was individually rational.

The forged lever opener wasn't better engineering for its era. It's better engineering, period. It just couldn't survive a market that prices durability at zero.

#### The bottom of Lake Como is 200 meters below sea level, and the reason is that the Mediterranean Sea once evaporated almost completely.

Around 5.5 million years ago, tectonics sealed the Strait of Gibraltar. Cut off from the Atlantic, the Mediterranean dried into a salt desert more than a kilometer below today's coastlines. Geologists call it the Messinian Salinity Crisis.

With the sea gone, every river flowing south off the Alps suddenly had a kilometer of extra elevation to fall. They responded the way rivers do: by cutting. The ancestral Adda carved a gorge so deep that its floor at Argegno sits roughly 700 meters below modern sea level. A canyon to rival anything in Arizona, sliced into the southern Alps.

Then Gibraltar breached. The Atlantic roared back in what may be the largest flood in Earth's history, refilling the entire Mediterranean in a geologic instant. The drowned canyons silted up.

The ice ages finished the job. Glaciers flowing off the Alpine ice sheet bulldozed through the half-buried gorge, widened the V into a U, and dumped terminal moraines at Como and Lecco that sealed the valley shut. When the ice retreated, water filled the trench. 425 meters deep, the deepest lake in the Alps.

So the postcard you're looking at is a flooded canyon inside a flooded canyon. Carved once by a vanished sea, carved again by vanished ice, and now lined with villas because the same violence that made it left slopes too steep to build towns on.

#### The Model Collapse paper everyone keeps citing tested a scenario that has never happened on the real internet.

Read the methodology. The researchers trained each model generation almost entirely on the previous generation's output and deleted the original human data. That's the setup that produced collapse. Nobody runs that setup. AI labs scrape and accumulate. Old human data stays in the corpus forever.

A Stanford and MIT team tested the realistic version in 2024. Keep the real data, add synthetic data on top, train the next generation. Collapse disappeared. Performance plateaued instead of degrading. They published it under the title "Is Model Collapse Inevitable?" and the answer was no.

Then the labs went further. Microsoft's phi models were trained heavily on deliberately generated synthetic textbooks and punched far above their size. Every frontier lab now distills larger models into smaller ones on purpose. Synthetic data went from contamination to core technique in about 18 months.

And the "ChatGPT feels dumber" intuition has a boring explanation: labs serve cheaper, faster model variants to manage inference costs. That's a serving decision, not poisoned training data.

The real consequence of this research is economic, not apocalyptic. Once labs understood that verified human data is the irreplaceable input, it got priced like one. Reddit licenses its archive to Google for $60M a year. Publishers sign nine-figure deals. Pre-2022 text is becoming the low-background steel of the internet: valuable precisely because nothing made after the contamination date can replace it.

The internet isn't collapsing into mush. Human data just stopped being free.

#### A Swiss lever escapement is one of the hardest mechanical systems you can ask software to fake, and most people have no idea why.

The escapement is the heart of a mechanical watch. A balance wheel oscillates 5 to 10 times per second, and each swing has to unlock a pallet fork, release one tooth of the escape wheel, receive a tiny impulse of energy back, and lock again. The geometry tolerances on real ones are measured in microns. Get the pallet angles slightly wrong and the watch doesn't run badly, it doesn't run at all.

Simulating it correctly means modeling impulsive contact, not smooth motion. The escape wheel tooth slides along the pallet jewel's impulse face, transfers energy in about 1 millisecond, then the system free-wheels until the next collision. Physics engines hate this. Discrete time steps blow straight past millisecond-scale contact events, so naive simulations either explode or quietly cheat by animating the motion instead of computing it.

That's the actual test buried in this demo. Anyone can make gears spin in Three.js. A mechanically accurate escapement means the tick emerges from the collision math rather than being keyframed. The model had to know horology, contact dynamics, and numerical integration tricks like substepping, then compose all three in one pass.

Watchmakers spend years learning to adjust this mechanism by hand. The training data contained enough of their knowledge to rebuild it in a browser.

#### The best thing you can do for your phone battery this summer costs nothing and takes ten seconds. Stop letting it sit face-up in the sun.

Phones are built to run in a narrow band, roughly freezing up to about 35°C. A dark screen in direct sunlight blows past that fast, because the glass soaks up light as heat while the processor is already warm. Hit the ceiling and the phone defends itself: it throttles to cool down, dims, and if it's still climbing, force-quits with a temperature warning. That's the dead afternoon you've seen.

The afternoon is the small problem. Heat works on two clocks. The one you feel is the black screen today. The one you don't is permanent. Heat is the single biggest cause of lithium battery aging, so the phone that bakes on a table every summer is the one holding 80% charge by year two while your friend's still holds 95%.

Apple and Samsung publish the same limits and the same advice. Keep it cool and it lasts. Cook it and it quietly loses capacity you never get back.

The fix is the whole point. Flip it face-down so the dark screen stops drinking light, slide it into shade, or drop anything over it. A drink menu works.

Your battery doesn't die of old age. It dies of heat.
