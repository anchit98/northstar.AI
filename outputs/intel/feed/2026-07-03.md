#### The word is "overmorrow," and the reason you've never heard it is a better story than the word itself. One man put it in English. Myles Coverdale, translating the first complete printed English Bible in 1535, hit the word "übermorgen" while working from Luther's German translation and needed an equivalent. German had one. Dutch had one. English didn't. So he wrote "ouermorow" into the Book of Tobit and moved on. It never caught on. The OED citation trail is almost comically thin: Coverdale in 1535, then near silence for centuries. When the King James translators standardized English biblical language in 1611, they skipped it. The KJV handed English hundreds of phrases people still use daily. Overmorrow missed the cut, and in a country where most people heard the Bible read aloud every Sunday instead of reading it, missing that one book meant extinction. Nearly every other major language kept theirs. German übermorgen. Dutch overmorgen. Japanese asatte, plus shiasatte for three days out. Hindi parson, which does double duty for the day before yesterday too, with context deciding which direction in time you're pointing. English lost the mirror word as well. "Ereyesterday" meant the day before yesterday, also appears in Coverdale, and survives today only as a regionalism in parts of Ireland and Scotland. So people aren't failing to know this word. The OED marks it obsolete. What's circulating is a 500-year-old translation experiment that the language already voted down once, getting a second election on the internet.

#### A CPO who used to manage hundreds of people now runs a product team of nine. Five PMs, four designers. It ships more than any of her bigger orgs ever did. Same title as five years ago, almost none of the same work. When one person can carry a feature start to finish, every extra body stops being capacity and starts being drag. Competitive write-ups, stakeholder briefs, research synthesis, manual CRM entry. You build the agent that monitors the competitor once, then you watch it. You set the system up instead of running it by hand every Monday. What's left for the human is the part an agent can't fake. Knowing what to build, for whom, and why it matters to the business. On her hardest feature this year, the agent handled the code and the design fine. The bottleneck was the content, the judgment on what the user and the firm actually needed. That call is the whole job now. So the field is splitting on judgment. Seniority stopped being the line. PMs who own a problem end to end keep getting handed more. The ones whose value was mostly coordinating other people are finding that part handled by the system. Smaller team, bigger output. The bar to be on it went way up.

#### The US government banned the world's best AI model for 19 days. It came back this week scoring near the bottom of the leaderboard at the exact thing it was best at. The explanation is sitting in plain sight, and it has nothing to do with intelligence. To get un-banned, Anthropic trained a filter to block one behavior: identifying software vulnerabilities. Then a benchmark asked the model to diagnose broken TypeScript. To a security filter, those are the same cognitive act. Find the flaw in the code, explain why it fails, show how to trigger it. Debugging is vulnerability discovery pointed at your own repo. Look at how the drops distribute. Debugging fell 60 points. Refactoring, which requires understanding failure modes to preserve behavior, fell 35. Hallucination checks, mostly reading code and verifying claims, fell 14. The filter fires in proportion to how much each task resembles exploit work. That gradient is the tell. Now run the math on Anthropic's side. They report over 95% of sessions never trigger the filter. That figure averages across all users, most of whom are drafting emails and planning trips. Developer workloads are the concentrated tail. A filter firing on 5% of total traffic can fire on the majority of debugging traffic and both statements stay true. One number still refuses to fit. Blocked requests get answered by Opus 4.8, a frontier coding model in its own right. If every blocked task got a clean Opus answer, the floor would be Opus's score. A 25.9 sits near the bottom of the entire leaderboard, which suggests the benchmark counts the mid-task model swap itself as a failure. So this score measures three things at once: the model's raw ability, the filter's trigger rate, and the benchmark's tolerance for rerouting. Only one of those is the model. The government ordered Anthropic to block vulnerability discovery. Debugging is vulnerability discovery. The benchmark is measuring the compliance.

#### Bridgewater just published numbers that should make every frontier lab nervous. The world's largest hedge fund tested Gemini, Claude, and GPT on six document filtering tasks its investors do every day. Naive prompts scored around 50%. A coin flip. Expert-written prompts pushed accuracy to 78%. Investors needed 80% before they'd trust the system in their workflow, and no frontier model cleared it. GPT 5.4 cost 43% more than 5.2 and was barely more accurate. So they fine-tuned Qwen3-235B on Tinker instead. 84.7% accuracy. 29.8% fewer mistakes than the best frontier model. At 1/14th the inference cost. The smartest part is buried in the middle of the paper. Their vendor-labeled training data was riddled with wrong labels, and expert labeling costs too much to run on everything. Their fix: train a model on the noisy dataset, then run it back over its own training data. Any example the model disagreed with got routed to senior investors, because either the example was genuinely hard or the label was wrong. The model's own confusion became a detector for bad labels. Prompting hit a ceiling for a structural reason. A prompt captures only the judgment an expert can put into words. Twenty years of taste about which central bank memo actually signals a rate move doesn't compress into instructions. It transfers through labeled examples. Every institution sitting on decades of expert decisions just learned that those archives can train a model that beats the frontier at their specific job. The alpha was in the filing cabinet the whole time.

Rename the overhang as inventory and the capex bill flips from liability to product line. Same GPUs, same data centers, $160B repricing in one session. The word did damage in the other direction too. CoreWeave and Nebius each fell about 12%, on the day their largest customer implied it might have spare capacity to sell against them. Meta is simultaneously their biggest tenant and, as of today, their newest competitor. Nvidia, Broadcom, AMD, and TSMC all dropped on the same logic: if Meta has extra, maybe it orders less. Here's the actual play. Zuckerberg said the quiet part in May: if the buildout overshoots, selling compute is the escape hatch, and knowing the escape hatch exists is what lets him keep spending. The cloud business isn't a business yet. It's downside insurance that lets Meta run capex harder than any company without one. SpaceX ran the identical move a month ago, leasing Colossus capacity to Anthropic and Google. The pattern across the category: build more than you need, and the overflow becomes either your moat or your revenue. Either way you never have to slow down. The companies that can't stop buying compute just found a way to get paid for buying too much.
