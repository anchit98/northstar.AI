## PM Thought Leaders

### Aakash Gupta

#### The model is the least important part of getting AI to work for you.

An OpenAI PM running international growth showed me where the real leverage lives: the harness. Three layers most people skip entirely.

Layer one is connectors. He wired up seven or eight scattered dashboards once (Databricks, Tableau, the competition dash) and then stopped asking generic questions. "How is weekly active growth doing" returns garbage when you have a consumer table and a business table that look identical and mean different things. He points the agent at the exact dashboard, the exact metric, every time. Specificity is the whole game.

Layer two is permissions. Read tasks and synthesis run on full autonomy. Anything headed to another human gets his eyes first. He has the agent draft his weekly update and never send it. Treat permissions as a single on/off switch and you either babysit work that should be automated or ship a hallucination straight to your VP.

Layer three is skills. Reusable workflows written once, triggered by name. His team built one for experiment reviews: point it at the stat signal and it writes the hypothesis, monitors the run, and drafts the recommendation. The best skills come from whoever got most tired of doing the work by hand. Could be an analyst, could be a PM. The person who cares most ships it.

Here's what clicked for me. He stopped writing PRDs and started shipping prototypes to 70-80%, then handing engineers the last mile. That only works because the harness was already built. The prototype is downstream of the plumbing.

An agent without a harness is a chatbot you re-explain your job to every morning. Set all three layers and it stops being a thing you open and becomes a thing that runs while you sleep.

The PMs pulling ahead in 2026 share one habit. They built the harness before they argued about the model.

#### A PM at OpenAI wrote a markdown PRD on Monday and shipped a deployed pull request by Friday. No engineer touched the keyboard.

The reason this worked tells you everything about where software bottlenecks actually moved.

Ryan Lopopolo's team built a million-line codebase with zero human-written code. Code generation is free. Validation is the new constraint: proving the code actually does what the user needs. That single shift rewrote every job on the product team.

PMs went from writing Google Docs that sit in a queue to writing PRDs as executable assertions. Product taste encoded in failing tests. The agent must pass the PM's tests to ship. The PM now controls what gets deployed directly, without waiting for an engineer to interpret their intent.

Designers went from Figma handoffs that get lossy-translated into CSS to owning full painted-door UI flows. No-op backends, real instrumentation. Usage data decides what earns a real backend. When the designer tried building a full backend feature, it spaghetti'd and they reverted. Painted doors first. Data decides.

Engineers went from value = commits to value = leverage. Tests, docs, CI rules, review agents, observability. Every agent mistake becomes a permanently prevented class of failure. The codebase grades itself.

The PM role gained the most. Old world: every product insight passed through a lossy handoff chain before it became code. Now a markdown file compiles directly into a shipping pull request. The PM went from furthest from the artifact to closest to the output.

#### Ancient bread fermented for hours, sometimes overnight. Most bread you buy now goes from flour to packaged loaf in about three and a half hours. The protein in the wheat is nearly identical to a century ago. What changed is what the dough does before it hits the oven.

A USDA survey of wheat from the 1920s to today found gluten content basically flat. The dwarf varieties from the green revolution didn't load up on it. So the "modern wheat is juiced with gluten" idea doesn't survive contact with the data.

What did change is two specific things. Vital wheat gluten, a concentrated gluten powder, gets added to industrial dough to fake the structure that slow fermentation used to build, and US consumption of it has roughly tripled since 1977. At the same time, the fermentation itself got deleted. Long proofing breaks down fructans, a fast-fermenting carb in wheat, by up to 90 percent at around 4.5 hours of rise. Drop the proof to an hour and those fructans ride into the loaf intact.

That's the quiet part: most people who blame gluten are reacting to fructans instead. Fructans pull water into the gut and ferment quickly, which is the bloating and the gas. The crowd that swears they "can eat bread in Europe" are usually eating the long-fermented version that already digested its own fructans before they ever touched it.

True celiac is a separate thing, and it genuinely rose. Serum from a 1950 Air Force base compared against samples in 2006 showed celiac markers climbing from 0.2 to 0.9 percent. Close to a fivefold jump in actual immune response, not just more testing.

The grain held steady for a hundred years. The processing stripped out the slow step that made it digestible, then added concentrated gluten back in to compensate. Most people are reacting to a faster loaf. A smaller group reacts to the protein itself, for reasons still being worked out.
