#### Your eyes have never seen an object. They've only ever seen light: edges, contrast, shadow, glare. Your brain takes that mess of effects and constructs "castle" or "face" after the fact. Sharp vision covers about 2 degrees of your visual field, roughly a thumbnail held at arm's length. Everything else is inference. The problem is the inference comes with labels. By age 8 you've compressed every object into a stored symbol: eyes are almonds, the sun is a circle with rays. When you sit down to draw, the symbol hijacks your hand, and you copy a cached icon from childhood instead of the thing in front of you. That's why the classic art school trick works: turn the reference photo upside down. Recognition fails, the labels can't load, and people who "can't draw" suddenly produce accurate work. A 1997 study on drawing errors found the bottleneck was misperception, not motor skill. The hand was never the problem. It also explains why AI image generation lapped computer graphics. CGI spent 50 years modeling objects: meshes, textures, physics engines. Diffusion models skipped objects entirely and learned the statistics of light across 5 billion photos. The approach that only draws effects beat the approach that draws objects in about 3 years. Artists figured this out a century before the machines did. There are no objects in an image. There is only light, and the people who learned to draw it.

#### Most AI builders find out their agent is broken from an angry user, not from their own testing. I'm running 16 agents in one system plus a separate recommendation engine, and every one of them looked fine in testing before someone told me it hallucinated or picked the wrong tool. The problem had nothing to do with the prompt. I had no way to see what the agent was actually doing, step by step, every tool call, every decision, before it handed back an answer. One agent was supposed to pull a company's tech stack straight from the job posting. It said React. The posting said Python. I never would have caught that without watching what the agent actually did. Once I could see it, I could grade it. Fixed and re-tested, it dropped under 2%. The whole loop took about 20 minutes, and it runs on its own now. Prompting was the skill that mattered in 2024. Watching what the model actually does is the skill that matters now. Instrument it.
