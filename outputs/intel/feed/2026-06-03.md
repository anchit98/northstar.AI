#### Concrete this strong cracking like drywall comes down to one number: its tensile strength.

A normal pour handles around 4,000 psi in compression. In tension it gives out near 400 psi. That 10-to-1 weakness is what water exploits.

Hydrostatic pressure bends a wall rather than crushing it. Water pools behind the wall, pushes sideways, and the dry face goes into tension. The wall hits its tensile limit while its compressive strength sits almost untouched.

The force gets brutal because it scales with the square of depth. Water pushes 62 pounds per square foot for every foot down. At 10 feet, that's 624 psf at the base and roughly 3,100 pounds on every linear foot of wall. Double the water depth and the load quadruples.

That's why you see cracking and bowing across the face. The wall is being pried open from the dry side as the water leans into it.

The real failure happened before the concrete ever cured. Someone undersized the drainage, skipped the waterproofing membrane, or let the backfill saturate. The concrete did exactly what concrete does. Water found the one direction it couldn't defend.

#### This is real and it has been running for almost 900 years.

#### A camel foot is basically a self-inflating snowshoe.

Press down and the fat pad under those two toes flattens and spreads, so the harder a 600kg animal pushes, the more sand it recruits to carry the load. No hooves, no hard edges, just a cushion that grows under force.

Pressure is force divided by area. A horse drops that same weight onto four small hard hooves and punches straight through loose sand. The camel takes an identical load and spreads it across a soft pad that expands on contact, which is why it floats where heavier-footed animals dig themselves into a pit.

The heat defense runs on the same hardware. That pad is fibrous tissue packed with fat, and fat barely conducts heat, so it sits between living tissue and ground that can run past 65C and refuses to pass the temperature through.

Here's the part the snowshoe story skips. When researchers put camels on pressure plates, the fat pad's measured job was evening out force across the foot and absorbing impact. The popular explanation fixates on sinking. The data points harder at shock absorption.

Evolution solved suspension, thermal insulation, and ground pressure with one squishy pad. Engineers usually need three separate parts for that.

#### Most "self-updating" AI memory is one sentence in a config file asking the model nicely to remember. That is not a system, and it is why your context library goes stale by month three. You have watched it happen.

I spent the last month testing the two best memory builds out there. Garry Tan's GBrain captures everything you say to an always-on phone agent and consolidates it overnight. Advisory is the floor. A rule in CLAUDE.md that says "capture as you work." The agent follows it most of the time and silently drops it as context fills. Useful for nuance, and the exact reason every notes system rots. If your entire self-update story is a config rule, it will go stale, and you already know it will.

Deterministic is the part that turns a hope into a property. One check fires the instant a file is written and blocks any claim with no source attached. #### Caleb is right, and the math is worse than most people think.
