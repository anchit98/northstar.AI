#### A turbo parts company with a tiny niche audience just ran the highest-ROI ad it will run all year, and the entire production budget was one fake memo.

Burger Motorsports makes plug-and-play performance tuners for turbocharged BMWs and Audis. Founded in 2007, sells to a narrow slice of car enthusiasts. Nothing about that business says "goes viral."

Plenty of brands bolt themselves onto a trending event and get scrolled past. This one stuck because of a fact most marketers wave off: the person who mods his BMW with an aftermarket tuner and the person counting down to GTA 6 are the same person. Those two audiences are close to one circle.

So the joke spoke past the broad internet and straight to their actual buyer, and that buyer's whole subculture carried it for them. Free reach, exact targeting, because the joke itself screened for the customer. If you didn't get it, you were never the target.

The format did the rest. A fake internal HR notice reads like a leak, so people forward it. Ads get scrolled past; documents get screenshotted. They dressed a promotion up as a company memo and let the platform handle the distribution.

The cheapest channel a company owns is an inside joke its customers are already in on. Most brands can't run it, because they don't know their customer well enough to write the joke. This one did.

#### Your phone stopped shattering thanks to a trick that sounds backwards. The screen surface is permanently squeezing itself, and since a crack can only spread when glass gets stretched, a drop has to overcome that built-in compression before it can even begin.

Here's how they engineered it in. Raw glass is covered in microscopic surface flaws, and any flex pulls those flaws open under tension. So Corning bathes the panel in molten potassium salt. The larger potassium ions force their way into the surface and shove out the smaller sodium ions already sitting in the glass. That overcrowding locks the entire outer layer into compression, hundreds of microns deep. A crack now has to fight its way through that squeeze before it can grow at all.

Apple stacked a second defense on top in 2020. Ceramic Shield grows nano-ceramic crystals directly inside the glass, each one smaller than a wavelength of light, so the panel still reads as perfectly clear while the crystals sit in the path of any crack and deflect it. Apple rates the result at four times the drop survival of the glass before it.
