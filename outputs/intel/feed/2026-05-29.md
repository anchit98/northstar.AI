#### The Uncharted movie took 14 years and 7 directors to reach a theater. Mark Wahlberg was originally cast as Nathan Drake. He aged out of the role and got moved to the sidekick.

Sony first acquired the rights in 2008, one year after the game launched. David O. Russell signed on to direct. Then quit. Neil Burger attached. Then quit. Seth Gordon, Shawn Levy, Dan Trachtenberg, Travis Knight. All attached. All gone. Each one brought a new script. Each new script required a new vision. The project burned through writers, budgets, and release dates for over a decade.

Tom Holland got the role because he walked into Sony and pitched himself for James Bond. Sony said no, but offered him Nathan Drake as a compromise. He was 20 years old. By the time cameras rolled in 2020, Holland had become the biggest young star on the planet off Spider-Man, and the movie that couldn't get made suddenly had a $120 million budget and a guaranteed opening weekend.

Critics gave it 41% on Rotten Tomatoes. Audiences gave it 90%. It made $407 million worldwide on a movie that most of Hollywood had written off as permanently dead. Three years later it hit Netflix and immediately climbed to the most-watched title of the week. 10 million views.

The gap between 41% critics and 90% audience is the entire story of why video game adaptations kept failing for 30 years. Studios kept trying to make films that impressed reviewers. Players just wanted to watch characters they love do cool things on a big screen. Sony stopped trying to make art and made a theme park ride. The audience said thank you.

#### That brick is roughly 1,276 pages and 460,000 words. The length is an artifact of how Dumas got paid.

Monte Cristo wasn't written as a book. It ran in the Journal des Débats from August 1844 to January 1846, seventeen months of installments, while readers waited for the next piece the way people wait for a season finale now.

Serial writers in 1840s France got paid by volume. More words, more installments, more money, plus a cut tied to keeping the paper's subscribers hooked week after week. Dumas had every incentive to stretch. He also worked with a collaborator, Auguste Maquet, who built the plot skeletons so Dumas could keep the output enormous across multiple serials running at once.

So the page count people meme about traces straight back to a payment model. He was running a content business with a publishing deadline every few days, optimizing for length and cliffhangers because that's what the contract rewarded. The classic was a high-volume serialized product first and a bound novel second.

The same structure that forced him to stretch also forced a hook into the end of nearly every installment. Two centuries later that pacing is why people still can't put the brick down.

#### Your AI sidebar asks users to describe things the product already renders. A solo dev and Google just shipped the fix for that the same week.

Farza built Clicky: an AI that lives next to your cursor, sees your screen, and points at the answer. You hold a hotkey and speak. A blue triangle flies to the exact button you need. You never open a new window. You never describe your screen.

Two days later, Google DeepMind published research on reimagining the 50-year-old mouse cursor with AI. Same hypothesis: put intelligence at the cursor instead of the chatbox.

When independent convergence happens this fast, the hypothesis is worth taking seriously. The chatbox may have been a transitional interface.

I wrote about what to build next and created a 30-minute audit toolkit. Three shifts every PM should evaluate on their roadmap:

Shift 1: Long Generation → Micro-Interaction. Your AI takes 30 seconds to generate an answer. The cursor layer delivers it in eight seconds while the user keeps working. Build for frequency. The most valuable AI interactions are the shortest ones.

Shift 2: User Manages → System Absorbs. Your user picks a model, names a thread, pastes their screen. The cursor layer reads context automatically and routes the query. Every setting you expose is friction.

Shift 3: Guide → Doer. Your AI explains how to click the button. The cursor layer clicks the button while the user watches. Start as teacher, graduate to executor.

The audit scores each feature using Frequency × Time Saved × Gap ÷ Effort. When I ran it on a fictional B2B SaaS, the highest-scoring item was a one-sprint embedded fix. The ambitious cursor-layer rebuild scored 4.5x lower. The effort denominator changes everything.
