#### Silver polish works by grinding off a microscopic layer of your actual silver every time you use it. The foil trick does the opposite. It runs the tarnish reaction in reverse and puts the silver back on the piece. Tarnish is silver sulfide. Silver pulls sulfur straight out of the air, and hydrogen sulfide at just a few parts per billion is enough to darken a spoon over months. That black layer contains your silver, chemically locked to sulfur. Aluminum bonds to sulfur more aggressively than silver does. Drop both metals into hot water with baking soda and salt and you've built a battery in a bowl. The baking soda and salt turn the water into an electrolyte that carries current, the heat speeds up ion traffic, and electrons flow from the foil into the silver. Sulfur atoms let go, migrate across the water, and bond to the aluminum instead. Pure metallic silver stays behind exactly where the tarnish was. The faint rotten egg smell while it works is a trace of hydrogen sulfide escaping. The reaction announcing itself. Antique dealers can spot pieces that got polished too often because the hallmarks wear soft and engravings lose their edges. Every polish session removes metal that never comes back. The foil method removes nothing. Tarnish forms over months, one sulfur atom at a time. A cereal bowl and pantry chemicals reverse it in five minutes.

I keep hearing anecdotes from people who used coding agents to reverse-engineer and automate devices in their homes. I think this is an interesting illustration of the impact of the reduced cost of writing code. Prior to agents, it was entirely possible to reverse-engineer home devices. The problem was the ROI - was it really worth all of that effort? More importantly, any experienced programmer knows that undocumented, unstable APIs like that may well change or break in the future.
