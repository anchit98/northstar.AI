#### The laser never removes your tattoo. Your immune system does, and that's the whole reason this took Wahlberg years instead of months.

The laser only shatters the ink into fragments small enough to move. Then macrophages swallow those fragments and carry them out through your lymphatic system, which has no pump. It drains slowly because nothing is pushing it.

That's why removal sessions sit 6 to 8 weeks apart. You're waiting on your own body to clear pigment one batch at a time.

So "5 to 7 visits" turning into "30+ over many years" isn't bad luck. You cannot buy your way past lymphatic clearance. Better laser, better clinic, more money, and the drainage still runs at the speed it runs.

Now look at the one tattoo he kept: Rhea's name on his ring finger. Fingers have the weakest circulation and slowest lymph drainage on the body. Ink there clears worse than anywhere else. The single tattoo he held onto sits in the exact spot that resists removal hardest.

The asymmetry is the real story. A tattoo is one afternoon and a few hundred dollars. Undoing it is years, dozens of sessions, thousands of dollars, the bacon grease feeling every single time, and even then you can be left with permanent ghosting that never fully lifts.

Easy to ink. Brutal to erase. Most permanent decisions run exactly like this. The tattoo is just the one you can see.

#### The honest answer: that dimple is there so the dairy can hand you exactly one gallon and not a drop more. Plastic jugs shrink by random amounts after they leave the mold, so two jugs off the same machine hold slightly different volumes of milk.

Fixing that by recutting a steel mold costs tens of thousands of dollars. So they don't.

Instead they bolt a small swappable insert inside the mold, and that insert is what stamps the circular dent. Pop in a bigger or smaller one and the jug's volume shifts by a few milliliters, dialing every bottle to an exact gallon without ever touching the expensive tooling.

The freezing story you've heard (the dent gives milk room to expand) is real, but it's a side benefit. The dent earns its keep by letting one mold hold legal fill volume across hot summer and cold winter runs.

The "it pops out when milk spoils" thing is mostly a myth. Harmless bacteria bloat a jug too, and plenty of spoiled milk never moves it, so it was never a real warning sign.

Here's the kicker: a gallon jug is about 60 grams of plastic, and Americans buy more than 6 billion gallons a year. Trimming fractions of a millimeter off every wall while still landing on an exact gallon is where the money is. That little dimple does both jobs at once.

#### Tadalafil went generic in 2018. At cost-plus pharmacies it runs about 15 cents a pill. The molecule has been a near-free commodity for seven years, which is why that same screenshot shows brand Cialis at $59.30 and the generic at $2.96 for the identical active ingredient.

So what has everyone actually been paying $40 to $958 a month for? The gate.

Hims and Ro built billion-dollar businesses sitting in front of a commodity pill and charging rent on the prescription: the doctor visit, the awkward conversation, the subscription lock-in. The drug was always cheap. Access to it was the product they sold.

Amazon just automated that gate down to a 10-minute consult and priced the pill at cost.

The math tells you who survives. Hims lives on the markup, because the pill margin is the entire model. Amazon lives on Prime renewals and the next reorder, so it can sell tadalafil at $2.96, lose nothing that matters, and still pull the customer in. A 15-cent commodity becomes a retention hook instead of a revenue line.

Tadalafil is just the test case. The same setup runs straight through finasteride, sildenafil, statins, SSRIs, metformin: every off-patent drug whose only real cost was the friction of getting prescribed it.

For seven years the cheap part was the drug and the expensive part was permission to buy it. Amazon just made the permission free.

#### This looks like cannibalism. The biology is the most extreme parental care evolution has ever built.

That golden coil is a Scolopendra, one of roughly 3,000 centipede species, and she spent the entire brooding stretch wrapped around those eggs without hunting or eating, guarding the clutch with her own body while she wasted down.

The young eating her is the design. Matriphagy converts her whole remaining body into one energy dense first meal, and broods that get it survive at far higher rates than broods that don't.

The part the caption skips: this goes past calories. In free living nematodes, researchers found the young pull oleic acid from the mother during matriphagy, and that fatty acid neutralizes a bacterial toxin that would otherwise stall larval development. Her body does double duty, fuel and chemical shield.

Some species push it further. In certain spiders the mother drums and vibrates the web to signal her brood, calling them up to feed when she's ready for it.

So she wasn't ambushed by her own offspring. She loaded her body with everything they needed to clear their first hours alive, then handed it over. That writhing pile is the highest return investment in the animal kingdom.

#### The Jin Mao Tower carved a 115-meter hole straight through the highest hotel on earth, and that empty column is the single most expensive decision in the building.

Everything about this tower runs on the number 8. 88 floors. 420.5 meters. Eight steel mega-columns carrying the load. A pagoda taper built in eighths. In Chinese tradition 8 is the prosperity number, so SOM and Adrian Smith engineered the entire geometry around it.

The spiral you're looking down starts on the 56th floor and climbs to the 87th: 28 ringed corridors, 27 meters across, hotel rooms stacked behind every ring facing outward while the center stays deliberately hollow.

Now think about what that costs. This is Lujiazui, some of the most valuable vertical real estate in Asia, and they deleted the core out of the most expensive 33 floors in the stack. Every floor of that void is leasable volume someone chose not to lease.

Because the void was the product. They put Cloud 9, the world's highest bar, on the 87th, and a Skywalk on the 88th, so you could stand at the very top and feel your stomach drop looking straight down it.

A normal developer maximizes floor area. In 1999 Shanghai the brief ran the other way. The building had to stand in a financial district that barely existed yet and announce that Pudong had arrived. The vertigo is the message.

A single berried female carries up to 20,000 eggs under her tail for nearly a year, fans them all out in one night, and statistically a single larva lives to grow up. The rest get eaten during the 4 to 6 weeks they spend drifting near the surface, defenseless, each one the size of a mosquito.

The math is why she makes so many. At 1 in 20,000, the only thing keeping the species alive is volume. Over her entire life she essentially replaces herself with one adult. Catch a female before she breeds, disturb that 1:1 balance even slightly, and the population starts sliding.

This is exactly what the hatchery in that clip changes. Raise the larvae in captivity through the weeks where they normally get devoured, then release them, and survival climbs roughly 1,000x. Same animal, same eggs. The only variable that moved is whether something eats them in week three.

A quiet morning in a tank is one of the most lopsided lotteries in nature, with the odds being rewritten right there on camera.

#### This guy's family thinks he's crazy for collecting cans. The math says he's collecting them in the wrong state.

32 aluminum cans make one pound. Indiana pays scrap value: about 55 cents per pound, which works out to roughly 1.7 cents per can.

Michigan, one state over, pays 10 cents per can through its bottle deposit law. Same aluminum, same effort, 6x the payout.

That pile looks like 10,000+ cans. In Indiana, that's roughly $170. In Michigan, the same pile pays $1,000.

Only 10 out of 50 states have bottle deposit laws. Michigan, Oregon, and Connecticut pay 10 cents per can. California and five others pay 5 cents. The remaining 40 states pay pure scrap value: less than 2 cents per can.

Michigan's 10 cent deposit produces a 90% container return rate. States without deposits sit around 35%. At 10 cents, every can on the ground is worth picking up. At 1.7 cents, most people walk past.

His family sees a guy hauling cans for pennies. What they're actually seeing is a 40-state policy failure priced into one man's truck bed.
