#### There were six members of NSYNC. Five of them knew it.

Lou Pearlman wrote himself into the band's contracts as a sixth member, collecting a full share of the group's already reduced 49% of earnings. Then he collected again as their manager. Then again as their record label. Then again on merchandise. Four separate cuts from the same revenue stream, layered so the teenagers signing couldn't trace where the money went. The guys were making $35 a day while their first album sold 10 million copies.

"No Strings Attached" sold 2.4 million copies in its first week. March 2000. No album had ever done that. No album would do it again for 15 years, until Adele. It moved 9.4 million copies by December. And the title was the tell: NSYNC had just escaped Pearlman's contracts through a lawsuit. The album name was the declaration of independence.

Pearlman didn't need the band's money anymore. He'd already used their fame to build something bigger. He told investors he ran an airline, a film studio, a savings program backed by FDIC insurance. None of it existed. Trans Continental Airlines didn't own a single airplane. He forged bank statements, faked insurance certifications, and used NSYNC's celebrity as bait to convince regular people to hand over their life savings.

The scheme ran two decades. $300 million stolen from over 1,500 victims. Sentenced to 25 years. Died in prison in 2016 at 62.

The part nobody talks about: he created BOTH rival boy bands. Backstreet Boys and NSYNC. Same factory. Same contracts. Same extraction. He told The New Yorker his philosophy: "Where there's McDonald's, there's Burger King. Where there's Backstreet Boys, there's going to be someone else. Why not us?"

Five guys on a checkerboard stage performing the biggest album launch in history. A sixth collecting checks from a prison cell.

#### The engineering on this animal is absurd. 5.6 feet of wingspan carrying 2.6 pounds of body. The body itself is 7 to 11 inches long. That wing-to-mass ratio means the Giant Golden-Crowned Flying Fox generates lift so efficiently it can travel 25 miles in a single night foraging for figs.

No echolocation. Zero. Unlike every bat you've seen on a nature documentary, this thing navigates entirely by eyesight and smell. Megabats split from microbats roughly 58 million years ago, and the two groups evolved completely different sensory architectures. The golden-crowned flying fox has large forward-facing eyes that give it better night vision than most nocturnal mammals.

Here's where it gets wild. The scariest-looking animal in the Philippine archipelago is the reason the forests exist. These bats eat figs, digest the fruit, and deposit the seeds mid-flight up to a mile from the parent tree. Seeds that pass through a flying fox gut actually germinate at higher rates than seeds that fall naturally. The bat's digestive acids soften the seed coat just enough to accelerate sprouting.

Over 145 genera of plants in the Philippines depend on flying foxes for pollination and seed dispersal. The bats are rebuilding forests from the air every single night.

And the Philippines desperately needs it. Since 1900, forest coverage dropped from 70% to 20%. On the island of Negros, 4% of original forest remains. The one species that could help reverse that decline is endangered because locals hunt it for bushmeat and recreation. 90% of its old-growth roosting habitat is gone.

The creature that looks like it was designed to terrorize a village is actually the archipelago's most effective reforestation engine. And we're killing it.

#### Cooper gained 40 pounds in 10 weeks, and almost none of it was what you think it was.

The human body can synthesize roughly 0.25 to 0.5 pounds of actual contractile muscle protein per week under optimal conditions. That's the hard biological ceiling. Ten weeks of perfect training, perfect nutrition, perfect sleep gives you maybe 5 pounds of new muscle tissue. Cooper gained 40.

The other 35 pounds is where the physiology gets wild. Skeletal muscle stores glycogen, which is just chains of glucose packed into muscle cells. Each gram of glycogen pulls 3 to 4 grams of water in with it. A 185-pound man transitioning from normal eating to 6,000 calories of heavy carbohydrate loading can store an additional 500 grams of glycogen across his muscle tissue. That glycogen drags 1,500 to 2,000 grams of water along with it. Just from carb loading alone, Cooper's muscles were holding an extra 4 to 5 pounds of fluid they weren't holding before.

Then layer in the sodium. 6,000 calories of whole food means somewhere around 6,000 to 8,000 milligrams of sodium per day. Sodium increases extracellular water retention across every tissue in the body. His face gets fuller. His arms look thicker. His neck fills out. None of that is muscle. All of it reads as size on camera.

Then the intramuscular fat. When you eat in a sustained surplus, your body deposits fat between and within muscle fibers, a process called intramuscular lipid accumulation. The muscles don't get more defined. They get denser. Rounder. Exactly what "bear" looks like versus "ripped."

Cooper's trainer Jason Walsh had him doing twice-daily sessions starting at 5 a.m. Deadlifts, squats, bench, pullups. Cooper was pulling 425 pounds on camera and it was real weight. The training wasn't building 40 pounds of new tissue. The training was sending a signal to his body to partition the surplus toward muscle cells instead of just subcutaneous fat. The heavier you lift, the more your body prioritizes glycogen storage in the muscles that need it. Every heavy squat was a signal saying "store it here."

This is why actors can transform in 10 weeks and bodybuilders take years. They're playing completely different games. Bodybuilders are slowly synthesizing new contractile protein. Actors are hydraulically inflating existing muscle tissue with glycogen, water, and intramuscular fat. You can fill the tank in weeks. Building a bigger tank takes years.

Cooper asking "Am I too big?" after every take is the detail that tells you he understood the mechanism intuitively. The mass arrives fast when you're loading, not building. And it can overshoot just as fast.

#### Adidas just mass-released a $500 running shoe that is banned from every sanctioned race on the planet. And it sold out in minutes. The business logic is smarter than it looks.

World Athletics caps stack height at 40mm for road racing shoes. The Prime X Evo sits at 50mm. That extra 10mm of Lightstrike Pro Evo foam means the shoe physically cannot be worn in any official competition. Adidas printed that fact on the marketing materials. They're selling illegality as a feature.

The shoe helped Sibusiso Kubheka run 100km in 5:59:20, the first person to ever break six hours at that distance. A record that does not count in any official record book because the shoe violates the rules. Adidas spent years of R&D to break a record that technically doesn't exist.

Here's why that's the play. The Prime X Evo isn't the product. The Prime X Evo is the commercial for the product. Every running publication on Earth wrote about this shoe for free. Millions in earned media. The actual revenue shoe is the Adizero Adios Pro 4 at $250, which uses a legal version of the same foam technology, sits under the 40mm limit, and ships in real quantities.

The $500 price tag and lottery release aren't margin optimization. They're a scarcity engine that turns a running shoe into a streetwear drop. You're buying a concept car that happens to fit on your feet.

Adidas found the loophole: you can build whatever you want if you never claim it's for competition. The regulations assumed shoe companies wanted to win races. Adidas realized winning the marketing race pays better.

#### Heart disease kills more people than every cancer combined. Lilly just published Phase 1b data in the NEJM on a single injection that could prevent most of it. Permanently.

The science starts with a genetic accident. A small percentage of people are born with a broken PCSK9 gene. Their livers clear LDL cholesterol so efficiently that researchers tracking them over 15 years found up to 88% fewer coronary events. The effect was so large it looked like a data error.

The reason is timing. Statins lower your cholesterol at age 45. A broken PCSK9 gene lowers it from birth. Decades of low LDL compound into plaque that never forms in the first place. You can't reverse 40 years of arterial damage with a pill. You can prevent it entirely with a gene.

VERVE-102 is a base editor. Single IV infusion. One letter changed in your liver cells' DNA. The PCSK9 gene turns off permanently. Phase 1b results across 35 patients: 88% reduction in PCSK9 protein, 62% drop in LDL cholesterol at the highest dose. Durable at 18 months. The effect didn't fade.

Lilly paid $1.3 billion to acquire Verve Therapeutics for this program. The global statin market does roughly $16 billion a year selling daily pills that patients take for life. If a single infusion replaces 30 years of daily medication, Lilly didn't buy a drug. They bought the retirement plan for an entire drug class.

One shot to join the genetic lottery that most of the population lost.

#### This woman is making the single most rational financial decision in Florida right now, and everyone laughing at the video doesn't know the math.

FEMA says 1 inch of floodwater causes $25,000 in damage. One inch. The average flood insurance claim from 2020 to 2024 paid out $82,000. Hurricanes Helene and Milton combined for $78.7 billion in total damage across Florida and surrounding states. FEMA spent $8 billion just processing 65,000 flood claims from those two storms alone.

Now here's the part that makes this woman look like a genius. Florida launched a program called Elevate Florida, backed by $400 million in FEMA money, specifically designed to help homeowners lift their houses above flood elevation. Pinellas County received 3,678 applications. The number of homes where construction has actually started: zero. Not one.

She skipped the waitlist, paid out of pocket, and her house will be done by December. Meanwhile, her neighbors are still filling out forms.

Every foot above base flood elevation cuts insurance premiums by hundreds per year. Lifting a home can reduce flood insurance costs by 30 to 60% and increase resale value by 25%. A 24-foot lift probably runs $200,000 to $300,000. Two floods in one season, which is exactly what happened in 2024, would cost her more than that in damage, lost time, and uninsured losses.

She'll be watching the next storm surge from her living room window while the rest of the street is underwater and the Elevate Florida application portal is still buffering.

#### Paul Fitts published his law of pointing in 1954. Movement time equals a constant plus b times log base 2 of 2D over W. Distance to target divided by target width. Every mouse, touchscreen, and game controller built in the last 72 years is designed around this single equation.

For three years, every AI product violated it.

The chatbox lives in a different tab. A different window. Often a different app entirely. In Fitts's terms, the distance to the AI is effectively infinite. You can't click Claude from inside DaVinci Resolve. You have to leave DaVinci, navigate to a browser, find the right tab, click the text field, and start typing. Five steps. Each one adding distance. A 72-year-old equation would have predicted every one of them before the first chatbot shipped.

Clicky sets distance to zero. The AI lives at your cursor position. Hold two keys, speak, done. No navigation, no pointing, no window to find. The blue triangle flies to the exact pixel you need. Movement time collapses to the theoretical minimum that Fitts's equation allows.

Google's Magic Pointer does the same thing at the OS level. Wiggle the cursor over anything on screen and Gemini responds. The cursor IS the AI. Distance equals zero by definition.

Both teams independently optimized for the same variable in the same equation. Both arrived at the same minimum. And that minimum was computable from a paper published 72 years ago.

The five-step anatomy in the infographic is a Fitts's Law violation stretched across 2 minutes. Leave your work. Open a tab. Describe your screen. Read the answer. Find your place. Every step is distance. The cursor layer removes all of it in one collapse.

Fitts predicted the movement time. What his equation can't predict is what happens to behavior when distance drops to zero. You go from 3 questions a day to 30, because reaching for help just became free. The infographic says 15x faster. The behavioral shift is 10x more often. The frequency change matters more than the speed change, and that's the part no equation covers.

#### My biggest takeaways from @danshipper:

1. The future of work will happen inside Codex or Claude Code. Instead of putting AI into your SaaS tool, you’ll use your SaaS tools inside your favorite AI agents' in-app browser. Dan spends all his time in Codex now—writing documents, managing email, doing research, everything. He's using Google Docs, PostHog, and everything he needs within the agent's in-app browser. The agent can see what he’s doing, and has all of his context, so he and his agent collaborate quickly and super effectively.

2. Automation is a lie—every automation needs a human. Dan's company doubled in size this year despite being incredibly AI-forward. Why? Because in order to make automation work well, you need humans making sure everything keeps working. This is why benchmarks are misleading—they measure AI on problems we’ve already framed and can score, but there’s always a higher frame.

3. PMs will win the AI era. Marcus, a former PM who previously ran Axios’s writing product, joined Every after getting super AI-pilled. Now he runs their product Spiral, and ships faster than anyone on the team. He pairs technical knowledge with spiky product sense, deep user empathy, and an eye for what matters. Dan thinks any PM who gets really AI-native will be incredibly dangerous because the building is done for you—what matters is figuring out what to build and if it’s great.

4. Full-stack designers are becoming superheroes. Designers used to make beautiful interactions that engineers didn’t want to build or couldn’t execute properly. Now designers don’t need to hand things off; they can build it themselves. Designers are naturally creative people, and AI is the perfect tool for them because it lets them bring their vision to life without the traditional bottlenecks.

5. SaaS is not dead. In fact, Dan is bullish on SaaS stocks. When users bring their own AI (via Codex or Claude Code) to use SaaS products, the user—not the SaaS company—pays for tokens. This saves SaaS company’s margins. Since the agents need their own seats, Dan predicts that agents will create massive new demand for SaaS because there will be tons of agents using these products at high volume.

6. Every company will have one “super-agent” inside their Slack that every employee will use. Dan initially thought every employee would have their personal work agent, like a shadow AI org chart, but he’s completely flipped his view. He realized agents need humans who care about them. When someone gets tired of maintaining their personal agent, it becomes useless. The winning model is one forward-deployed engineer or AI-savvy person who maintains a company-wide agent (like Shopify’s River or Viktor), and then it trickles down to more specialized team agents as models improve and become less fiddly.

7. The AI job apocalypse is not happening, but you do need to evolve to stay relevant. Models make yesterday’s human competence cheap. But because everyone uses the same models, it all looks the same if you use it the default way; it becomes commoditized slop. Humans then take that frozen competence and use it to make something new and interesting for their specific situation. The key: “ride the models”—use them for everything you do, try new models when they drop, keep turning over rocks.

8. We will read way more AI-generated writing, and we will like it. Human writing is incredibly important for things that matter, but for internal docs, planning, and email, AI-generated is often better because most people are bad at writing strategy documents.

9. Build software for humans and agents to use together. The current model is building a CLI that an agent uses independently. Instead, you and your agent should be using the app together. This creates new design challenges—agents can make a billion requests in three seconds, so you need approval flows, inboxes that summarize what happened, logs, and easy rollback. 

10. Forward-deployed engineers are the new most essential role. The big model companies have teams of people managing their internal agents, and those teams aren’t going away. It’s different from traditional software building, and certain engineers love it. As models get better, this role will evolve—you’ll be managing more agents doing more things.
