#### The deadliest move a pilot can make with a dead engine is trying not to crash. The NTSB studied engine failures below 1,000 feet from 2013 to 2018. Pilots who accepted the crash and landed straight ahead died 8% of the time. Pilots who tried to turn back to the runway died 29% of the time. The split inside that number is even sharper. Over half the turnback attempts ended in a stall, and those stalls killed 70% of the pilots. Pilots who touched down wings level, still flying the airplane, died 4% of the time. The physics explains the gap. A controlled crash at 60 mph bleeds energy across hundreds of feet of ground. A stall converts the same crash into a near-vertical arrival, because the wing stops producing lift the moment airspeed drops below its limit. And every instinct in a panicking human, pull up, stretch the glide, rudder the nose around toward safety, is a stall entry. Which is why this landing looks so calm. Gliding between parked planes with a windmilling prop is the strategy with the 96% survival rate. The motto comes from Bob Hoover, the man Jimmy Doolittle called the greatest stick-and-rudder pilot who ever lived: "If you're faced with a forced landing, fly the thing as far into the crash as possible." Hoover lived it. Shot down in WWII, he escaped a German POW camp by stealing a Focke-Wulf fighter off the flight line and flying it to freedom. The airplane protects you for exactly as long as you keep flying it.
