#### The ocean is running a chemical alarm system and the waves just triggered it.

Those blue flashes are single-celled organisms called Noctiluca scintillans, each one between 200 and 2,000 micrometers wide. Inside every cell sit thousands of tiny granules called scintillons, loaded with a molecule called luciferin. When a wave physically deforms the cell membrane, protons flood from the acidic vacuole into the scintillon, dropping the pH from 8 to 6. That pH crash activates the enzyme luciferase, which oxidizes the luciferin into oxyluciferin and releases photons at exactly 475 nanometers. Blue light.

Each individual flash lasts about 100 milliseconds. What you're watching on that shoreline is millions of cells firing simultaneously because the same wave hit them all at once.

475 nanometers is the evolutionary sweet spot. Seawater absorbs red and yellow light within meters. Blue travels furthest through ocean water. These organisms didn't evolve blue for aesthetics. Blue is the only wavelength that functions as a signal underwater.

The biological purpose is called the "burglar alarm" hypothesis. A copepod starts grazing on a dinoflagellate. The cell flashes. That flash attracts a larger predator, which eats the copepod. The dinoflagellate weaponized its own distress into a revenge kill. Bioluminescence is a beacon that summons something bigger than whatever is eating you.

The molecules follow a circadian rhythm. Luciferin gets destroyed at dawn and resynthesized at dusk. Concentration peaks during four hours of deep night at roughly 10x daytime levels. The ocean saves its ammunition for darkness.

Heavy bioluminescent blooms also frequently signal nutrient overload. Excess nitrogen and phosphorus from agricultural runoff or sewage feed population explosions of exactly these organisms. The most beautiful thing the ocean produces is often a symptom of the water being sick.

#### Most AI products still run the same interaction: user describes their screen to a model that cannot see their screen.

That description step is where most AI interactions die. The user simplifies what they see. The model misinterprets. The answer comes back generic. The user does it manually and never comes back.

Clicky and Google's Magic Pointer both shipped the same week with the same fix. Four components, and the build order matters.

1. Screen Context. The AI sees what you see. No describing.
2. Voice Input. Speaking is 3x faster than typing. Deictic reference works because the AI already has the screen. "Fix this" resolves.
3. Spatial Response. The answer appears where you're working, not in a panel on the opposite side of your screen.
4. Agent Handoff. The AI that just pointed at the button now clicks it for you. Same interface, no mode switch.

Most products ship zero of these four. Your AI sidebar asks users to describe things the product already renders. That's a Stage 1 interaction hiding inside a Stage 2 product.

The audit question for every PM: what does the user currently describe to the AI that the system could just see?

#### RT by @lennysan: Nikita Bier: optimize for the 'aha' moment.

"If you can't demonstrate value in the first 3 seconds, it's over." 

Modern users have very short attention spans. If your onboarding requires too many steps or asks for personal data (like an email) before showing the product's value, user retention plummets.

"How do we expose them to an aha moment as fast as possible, in a memorable and iconic way?"

Nikita's example: Dupe originally had a shopping app with a bunch of features including one called Deal Hop which let users put in a product page and it’d help find the cheapest alternative online. They tried getting a very short domain (”dupe”) then created the ability for users to paste any existing URL on top of Dupe's URL and it’d immediately find cheaper alternatives.

Result: millions in ARR in under 60 days of launching. It was a blowout success.

Attention is a miracle. Don’t waste it. Consider every tap by a user on a mobile device. Cut features, improve speed, and be creative in using APIs and tools.

The memory shortage is causing a repricing of consumer electronics David Oks provides the clearest explanation I've seen yet of why consumer products that use memory are likely to get significantly more expensive over the next few years. The short version is that memory manufacturers - of which there are just three remaining large companies - have a fixed capacity in terms of how many wafers they can process at any one time. This fixed wafer capacity is then split between DDR - used in desktops and servers, LPDDR - used in mobile phones and low-energy devices, and HBM - used with GPUs. Until recently, HBM got just 2% of that wafer allocation. The enormous growth in AI data centers has pushed that up to an expected 20% by the end of 2026, and "a single gigabyte of HBM consumes more than three times the wafer capacity that a gigabyte of DDR or LPDDR does". Memory companies have learned from the extinction of their rivals that you should always under-provision rather than over-provision your fabricator capacity.
