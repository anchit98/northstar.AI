## PM Thought Leaders

### Aakash Gupta

#### This parasite is harmless the second it leaves a human body. It only turns dangerous after sitting in warm soil for one to two weeks. That delay is the entire reason washing your produce does almost nothing. Cyclospora comes out in stool as an oocyst, a thick-walled capsule that isn't infectious yet. It has to sporulate in the environment, 22 to 32°C, for 7 to 15 days before it can infect anyone. Most stomach bugs (salmonella, E. coli, norovirus) are contagious the instant they exit a body. This one has to sit outside and finish assembling itself first. By the time that finished oocyst lands on a strawberry or a bag of salad greens, it's a hardened spore built to survive weeks in open soil and water. Chlorine doesn't touch it. The CDC's line is blunt: no EPA-registered disinfectant has been shown to work on it. Commercial produce washes don't kill it either. A 2021 study found that rinsing berries under running water for a minute removes some of the oocysts, but Cyclospora is unusually sticky compared to other pathogens, so total removal isn't happening. Washing lowers the dose. It does not clean the food. Here's the part that flips the story. The shared language of a software project is not English or Python but it is the common understanding of what its concepts mean, where the boundaries are, which invariants matter, who owns what, and why the system has the shape it does. This language is rarely written down in one place.
