#### Lincoln is in the National Wrestling Hall of Fame. The real one in Stillwater, Oklahoma, which inducted him as an Outstanding American in 1992.

The famous 299-1 record is mostly frontier folklore. Historians at the Abraham Lincoln Presidential Library say it plainly: the win count is anecdotal and the true number is unknowable. What the Hall of Fame can actually document is the part that matters. One recorded loss in twelve years of competing.

This was "catch as catch can" wrestling, closer to a bar fight than a sport. No mat, no weight class, no referee. Lincoln had the build for it. Six foot four in an era when the average man cleared five-seven, with the arms and grip of someone who split rails for a living.

The match people still tell happened around 1831 in New Salem. His boss bet he could out-wrestle Jack Armstrong, the local champion and leader of a gang that liked to nail newcomers into barrels and roll them downhill. When Armstrong started fouling, Lincoln picked him up, slammed him, and knocked him out. Armstrong walked away respecting him. That fight built half of Lincoln's reputation in town.

The one loss came in 1832 during the Black Hawk War, to a fellow soldier, settling a dispute over a campsite that two units agreed to decide by sending their best man into the ring. Lincoln got thrown. He called it fair and went back to winning.

Stephen Douglas tried to use it against him in their 1858 debates, dismissing the wrestling years as an "amusing passage" in Lincoln's life. Douglas meant it as a knock. To the room, it played as a credential.

#### That diagram is labeling every part of the mold you can see. The part that makes "eating around it" dangerous is the part that's invisible.

Each hypha, the thread-like filament that forms the actual body of the mold, is 2 to 10 micrometers wide. Completely invisible to the naked eye. The fuzzy spot on your bread is just the reproductive structure. Those sporangia in the diagram are the equivalent of fruit on a tree. The organism itself is a sprawling network of microscopic filaments called mycelium that has already colonized the bread far beyond the visible spot.

Rhizopus stolonifer, the species in this diagram, is coenocytic. One continuous tube with no internal cell walls. A flowing river of cytoplasm with multiple nuclei, functioning as a single networked super-cell stretched into filaments that can penetrate 2.3 cm into adjacent bread slices within four hours. Zero visual signs on the surface.

Bread is almost uniquely defenseless against this. Water activity sits around 0.95 to 0.98, well above the 0.80 threshold where hyphae extend rapidly. Soft, porous, loaded with starches and sugars that act as ready fuel. The hyphae secrete enzymes that digest the bread externally and absorb the nutrients back through the filament walls. By the time you see color on the surface, the root system has already spread through the crumb in every direction.

Hard cheese survives because of density and low moisture, water activity around 0.85. Cut an inch around the spot and you're safe. The USDA makes the same distinction: hard cheese, trim it. Bread, throw out the entire loaf.

Toasting won't save it either. Some species produce mycotoxins that survive heat and remain invisible. You're eating the toxins whether you see the mold or not.

#### That scar on your arm is a battlefield, and the chemistry of how it forms is completely different from any other vaccine you've ever received.

Most vaccines inject dead or weakened pathogens into your muscle. Your immune system sees the threat, builds antibodies, done. No lasting damage to the tissue. The BCG tuberculosis vaccine does something radically different. It injects live Mycobacterium bovis bacteria directly into the top layer of your skin, the dermis, and then lets them multiply.

For the first six weeks, those bacteria are actively replicating at the injection site. Your immune system detects them and sends macrophages to engulf the invaders. T-cells get recruited to the area. Then something happens that no other routine vaccine triggers: your body builds granulomas. Those are organized clusters of immune cells that physically wall off the bacteria like a biological quarantine zone. The immune system can't fully kill every bacterium, so it builds a containment structure around them instead.

That containment war destroys tissue. The granulomas break down the dermis. A blister forms, then an open ulcer that weeps for weeks. The entire process from injection to final scar takes about three months. What you're left with is the structural aftermath of your immune system demolishing a section of its own skin to contain a live bacterial colony.

The wild part: 4 billion doses administered since 1921. 100 million newborns receive it every year. And the size of your scar correlates with how strong your immune response was. Studies in West Africa found that infants who developed a visible scar had half the mortality rate of infants who didn't. Not just from TB. From everything. The scar tissue itself became a marker that your immune system trained correctly.

That circular mark is the one vaccine scar that actually means something went right. Your body fought a live infection in a controlled space, won, and left the evidence on your skin for life.

#### The bacterium doing this is Leuconostoc mesenteroides CBA3656, and the mechanism is wild.

Its cell wall is covered in surface proteins that carry a net negative charge. Polystyrene nanoplastics, the most common type in food and water, carry a slight negative charge too. But the bacterium's surface has localized cationic patches, positively charged amino acid clusters, that create electrostatic attraction points. The plastic particles physically adsorb onto the bacterial cell wall the way iron filings stick to a magnet.

In a clean lab environment, CBA3656 captured 87% of nanoplastics in solution. The World Institute of Kimchi then tested it under simulated human intestinal conditions, with bile salts, pH fluctuations, digestive enzymes. Adsorption dropped to 57%. Still remarkable, because most biosorption strategies collapse entirely in the gut. The bile salts that destroy most bacterial binding capacity barely dented this strain.

The mouse trials are where it gets interesting. Germ-free mice given CBA3656 excreted more than double the nanoplastics in their feces compared to the control group. The bacteria weren't breaking the plastic down. They were physically wrapping around nanoplastic particles and riding them out through the intestine like a biological escort service.

Scale this against the problem. Estimates of human microplastic ingestion range from 0.1 to 5 grams per week depending on which study you trust. Nanoplastics, the smallest fraction, are the most dangerous because they're small enough to cross the intestinal barrier into the bloodstream and accumulate in the liver, kidneys, and brain. There has been no biological strategy to remove them once ingested. Every previous approach was either filtration before consumption or reducing exposure. This is the first mechanism that works after the plastic is already inside you.

And the delivery vehicle is a side dish that 96% of Korean households already eat daily.

#### Dirac couldn't get hired as an electrical engineer. A 19-year-old with a Bristol degree in 1921, during a post-war depression that had no use for him. So he stayed at Bristol and studied math for free because there was nothing else to do.

Two years later he got a fellowship to Cambridge. His advisor, Ralph Fowler, handed him proofs of an unpublished Heisenberg paper in August 1925. Dirac read it and realized the math resembled Poisson brackets from classical mechanics. Within months he had built an entirely new mathematical framework for quantum theory.

He published 11 papers before submitting his thesis. Eleven. Most PhD students struggle to publish one. Dirac had a body of work that constituted an entire theoretical foundation, and he still needed to package it into a dissertation to satisfy the degree requirements.

The thesis title tells you everything about the confidence level. When you title your PhD "Quantum Mechanics" at age 23, you are either delusional or correct. Dirac was correct. It was the first PhD thesis ever written on the subject.

Two years after that he wrote the Dirac equation, unifying special relativity with quantum mechanics and predicting antimatter before anyone had observed it. By 1932 he held the Lucasian Professorship of Mathematics at Cambridge. The same chair Isaac Newton held. He was 30.

Nobel Prize at 31. The youngest physics laureate at the time.

The entire arc from unemployable engineer to owning Newton's chair took 11 years. The field he named his thesis after is now the operating system of modern physics.
